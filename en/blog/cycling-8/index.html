Overview

Photo: Korea Tourism Organization Photo Gallery (Joan-myeon, Namyangju, Gyeonggi-do) · KOGL Type 1

The Bukhangang Bicycle Path starts in Namyangju, just east of Seoul, and follows the river through Cheongpyeong, Gapyeong, and Gangchon all the way to the lakeside city of Chuncheon — river on one side, mountains on the other, the whole way. Because it traces the old Gyeongchun rail line and the Bukhangang River, there’s almost no steep climbing, and the mix of riverside bike paths, boardwalks, and stretches built over converted old rail bridges keeps the scenery from ever getting old. Morning mist rising off the river and the fall colors on the pine-covered hillsides are especially gorgeous, which is why it’s consistently ranked among Korea’s most scenic bike routes.

There’s more than one way to ride it. First, as a one-day ride: leave early from the Namyangju side and you can reach Chuncheon with time to spare, grab dakgalbi for lunch, and take the train back. Second, it works well as part of a longer overnight tour — riders doing a full cross-country route from Seoul often spend the first night in Chuncheon before backtracking or connecting to another route. Third, it’s practically made for folding bikes paired with public transit: the Gyeongchun subway line and the ITX-Cheongchun train run right alongside the path, so if you’re running out of steam, you can just fold up your Brompton or mini-velo at the nearest station and hop on a train. Fourth, since the grade is so gentle, there are plenty of short, easy stretches for families and beginners to ride in pieces — like Ungilsan Station to Daeseongri, or Gangchon to Chuncheon.

The route starts at Bright Square (Balgeun Square) in Namyangju and ends at Sinmae Bridge in Chuncheon. Early on, it passes near Ungilsan Station and the Dumulmeori fork, hugging the Bukhan River on your right before running through Daeseong-ri and Cheongpyeong, then on to Gapyeong, Gangchon, and Chuncheon. Most of it is riverside dedicated bike path and boardwalk, so you’re kept safe and separate from car traffic — and crossing the bridges converted from old rail lines and passing through the tunnels gives you cool shade along with wide-open river views. Around Cheongpyeong Dam and the Gangchon gorge, the water and mountains line up into some of the route’s best photo spots.

This route is part of the national cross-country certification program. There are 4 certification centers in total: starting at the Balgeun Square center in Namyangju, then Saetteo Samgeori and Gyeonggang Bridge, and finishing up at the Sinmae Bridge center at the end. Hitting each certification center as you go naturally breaks the ride into segments, which makes it easier to pace yourself. The surface is flat for the most part, but in a few stretches near town entrances and on shared-use paths you’ll cross paths with pedestrians and casual riders, so it’s worth slowing down there.

The closer you get to Chuncheon, the more food options open up. As the finish-line city, Chuncheon is best known for dakgalbi (spicy stir-fried chicken) and makguksu (buckwheat noodles), and a lot of riders make a point of treating themselves to one or the other after finishing the route. Gapyeong, a stopover roughly midway, is known for dishes built around pine nuts — think pine nut noodles or pine nut porridge — while around Cheongpyeong and Gangchon you’ll find riverside specialties like freshwater fish dishes and spicy fish stew. Rather than naming specific restaurants, I’d just suggest keeping an eye out for these local specialties around each town’s main street or train station area.

As for supplies, convenience stores and cafés tend to cluster near stations and tourist spots like Daeseongri, Cheongpyeong, Gapyeong, and Gangchon. That said, some stretches of the riverside path run well away from any village, with no supply options for a while — so it’s safer to top up on water and snacks whenever you pass a station.

According to the Ministry of the Interior and Safety’s bike path database, this route has 4 certification centers, 20 restrooms, and 3 air pumps. There are zero water stations, though, so you’ll need to grab drinking water yourself at convenience stores, vending machines, or facilities near the certification centers. Twenty restrooms is generous for a riverside route, which makes it easy to plan rest stops, and knowing the 3 air pump locations ahead of time helps with keeping your tire pressure in check.

Since this is a certification route, it’s worth picking up a Bike Passport. Each certification center has a stamp on hand, so you can mark off your progress by stamping the matching box in your passport. Staffed centers sell passports on the spot, while unmanned booths just have the stamp for you to use. Honestly, collecting the stamps is half the fun of riding this route.

Since this route is a 70km one-way trek, you’ll need to figure out how you’re getting back to your starting point once you reach the finish line in Chuncheon.

The easiest option is taking the train back. From Chuncheon Station, you can hop on the Gyeongchun Line subway or the ITX-Cheongchun to head back toward Namyangju or Seoul. Regular trains and subways generally allow bikes on board, but if you’re riding a non-folding bike, there may be restrictions depending on the day of the week, time slot, or whether bike racks are available — worth checking before you go. High-speed trains like the KTX typically require folding bikes to be, well, folded and stowed as luggage. If you’re on a folding bike or mini-velo, this return option is especially painless.

You could also take the bus, but bike loading is often at the driver’s or route’s discretion, so unless you’re on a folding bike, it’s not really recommended. Useful stops near the start/end points include Chuncheon Station Overpass, Chuncheon Station Exit 1, Ungilsan Station, and Jinjung-ri·Joan-myeon Sports Park.

Finally, there’s the option of riding back the way you came (140km round trip) — a good fit if you’ve got the stamina and time for a full-on endurance ride. Rather than pushing through it all in one day, though, I’d recommend spending the night in Chuncheon and heading back the next day.

Most of the route runs along a riverside dedicated bike path, but near the town center and on a few shared sections, you’ll be sharing space with pedestrians and joggers. Slow down and give a bell ring as you pass through these stretches. In winter and early spring, watch out for ice that can linger on bridges and shaded deck sections — it’s easy to slip if you’re not paying attention. Tunnels and the old railway bridge sections stay dark even at midday, so a headlight and taillight are genuinely useful, and you should always ride with lights on during short-daylight seasons or night rides. Since there are no water stations along this route, pay extra attention to hydration — on hot days, make sure you fill up before heading into any stretch without resupply options.

Season, Weather, and Gear

Photo: Korea Tourism Organization Photo Gallery (Gapyeong-eup, Gapyeong-gun, Gyeonggi-do) · KOGL Type 1

The best seasons for this ride are spring and fall. Fall, especially, when the maples along the Bukhan River turn color, is arguably the single most scenic stretch of the whole route. Summer brings heavy heat and strong sun, and parts of the riverside have little shade, so an early morning start is worth planning for. Winter, meanwhile, ramps up the difficulty with icy patches and biting river winds. And no matter the season, that headwind riverside routes are famous for will drain your legs faster than you’d expect — pace yourself accordingly.

As for gear, don’t leave without a helmet, front and rear lights, and a flat-repair kit (spare tube plus a portable pump), along with plenty of water and snacks. There are no water stations along the way, so pack more water than you think you’ll need, and in summer, sun protection and a change of clothes will make the ride a lot more comfortable.

Foreign Traveler Tips

Alright, let’s move on to some practical tips for travelers.

If you’ve read this far, you’ll already have a decent sense of what to expect, but let me spell out a few things that are easy to overlook.

Transportation

Getting around isn’t hard once you get the hang of it, but it can feel confusing the first time. A T-money card (or its equivalent) will save you a ton of hassle — you can use it for buses, subways, and even taxis in some cases, so it’s worth picking one up as soon as you land.

Money

Cash still comes in handy at smaller shops and markets, but cards are accepted almost everywhere else. Don’t stress too much about currency exchange — just keep a bit of cash on hand for the odd stall or small eatery that doesn’t take cards.

Language

Don’t worry if you don’t speak the language. Translation apps work well enough for basic conversations, and a surprising number of people you’ll run into can get by in simple English, especially in touristy areas.

Etiquette

A little courtesy goes a long way. Taking your shoes off indoors, not being too loud in public transport, that kind of thing. Nothing you wouldn’t already guess, honestly — just pay attention to what the locals around you are doing and you’ll be fine.

That’s about it. Hope this helps make the trip a little smoother.

① Route name — The Bukhangang Bicycle Path (also signposted as the “North Han River Bicycle Path”) is romanized as Bukhangang Bicycle Path. It ends in Chuncheon, and the main station near the starting point is Ungilsan Station.

② A practical guide to the certification system — This route counts toward Korea’s national cross-country cycling Certification program. Your best bet for getting a Bike Passport is to buy one in person at a staffed certification center. The official reservation site is Korean-only, and between the language barrier and issues with foreign cards and Korean ID verification, booking online ahead of time is a hassle — so just plan on buying it on-site. The unmanned certification centers are the stamp booths shaped like red phone booths; just use the stamp inside to mark the matching box in your passport. Complete a section and you get a certification sticker, and if you finish the entire cross-country route, there’s a system for a completion certificate and medal too.

③ If you’re arriving without a bike — You can rent one locally at rental shops near tourist spots like Gangchon and Chuncheon. In the Seoul area, there’s the public bike-share system Ttareungi, which is easy to use since it supports an English app and foreign card payments. That said, Ttareungi is mostly geared toward getting around inside Seoul, so it’s better suited for short connecting rides than a long-distance route.

④ Map apps — Google Maps’ cycling directions are often inaccurate in Korea, or don’t offer a route at all. Instead, switch to bike mode on KakaoMap or Naver Map — the cycling routes are far more accurate. Both apps support an English interface, so it’s worth installing one before you set off.

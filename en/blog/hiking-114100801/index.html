North Han Mountain, Seoul: Granite Ridge Views a Stone's Throw from the City

Overview

Photo: Korea Tourism Organization photo gallery (Jeongneung-dong, Seongbuk-gu, Seoul) · KOGL

Bukhansan is one of the classic mountains right on the edge of the city, straddling central Seoul and Goyang in Gyeonggi Province. According to the data, the summit sits at around 834m, with the granite peak widely known as Baegundae marking the highest point. Even though it’s smack in the middle of the metropolitan area, it packs in rugged rock peaks and deep valleys, and you can reach the trailhead using nothing but the subway and city buses — so getting there couldn’t be easier. The data lists the top difficulty as “moderate,” but most of the routes that actually push up toward the summit involve a net elevation gain of over 600m, which is no joke on the legs. Add up all the trails in the park, split across several courses, and the total comes to 17.47km. There are no shelters, so whichever route you pick, this is a mountain you can comfortably do as a day hike.

Landscape and Terrain

Photo: Korea Tourism Organization photo gallery (Bulgwang-dong, Eunpyeong-gu, Seoul) · KOGL

Bukhansan is a classic granite mountain, and its defining feature is the line of smooth white rock peaks that run along the ridge. The three summits at its heart — Baegundae at the top, along with Insubong and Mangyeongdae — form a triangle, which is why the mountain has been known since ancient times as Samgaksan, the “three-horned mountain.” If you look at the main peaks recorded in the public data, you’ll find them rising all along the ridge: Sidanbong at 604m, Samobawi at 548m, Samgakjeombong at 545m, the summit of Uibong at 539m, and Jingwanbong at 531m. Beyond these, a scattering of lower peaks in the 388m–472m range branch off to form side ridges. So this isn’t a mountain you climb just to bag a single summit — the real fun is in ridge walking, linking one peak and stretch of old fortress wall to the next.

The terrain splits clearly into two worlds: the gentle forest paths down in the valleys, and the exposed rock slabs and steel-railing sections up near the summits. As you climb along a valley and then join a ridge, the view suddenly opens up and the granite peaks appear — so the higher you go, the more dramatically the landscape changes.

등산의 느낌

사진: 한국관광공사 관광사진 갤러리 (서울특별시 은평구 불광동) · 공공누리

From the trailhead up to the mid-slopes, the path winds through thick stands of oak and pine, so there’s plenty of shade and the walking is easy. On the valley routes you climb to the sound of running water, and you keep coming across waterfalls and spring-water spots along the way, which feels wonderfully refreshing. But as you get closer to the summit, the mood changes completely — you hit rocky climbs where you have to use your hands, exposed slabs of stone, and stretches with steel railings and stairs. Once you reach the ridgeline, the city of Seoul opens up before you, all the way over toward Dobongsan, and getting a view like this so close to downtown is the single biggest draw of hiking Bukhansan. Spring brings fresh green and azaleas down in the valleys, autumn brings the ridgeline foliage, and in winter the rock and ice send the perceived difficulty way up.

Course 1 — Trailhead → Summit

📏 8.09km ⛰️ 745m gain ⏱️ 3:16 🔁 Loop Difficulty ★★★★★ ⤓ GPX

Waypoints: Parking lot → Guksadang shrine → Restroom → Trailhead → Fork → Bukhansan National Park info board → Rest area → Emergency contact for wildfires and mountain accidents, 119 → Trail marker → Safe hiking, rest area with places to sit → Emergency contact for wildfires and mountain accidents, 119 → Viewpoint → 119 first-aid box → Wildfire fire extinguisher → Falling rock hazard

Getting to the trailhead: The start of the trail has a parking lot, the Guksadang shrine, and a restroom, so it’s an easy spot to get yourself sorted before heading up. If you’re taking public transit, the usual approach is to ride a city bus to a stop near the trailhead and walk in from there. I’d suggest checking the exact bus stops in your map app.

What it’s like & who it’s for: With a net elevation gain of 745m, this is about the biggest climb among the summit routes I actually measured. The early stretch is gentle, with forest paths and rest areas one after another, but the higher you go the more you run into sections with falling-rock hazards. So it suits anyone who’s confident in their fitness and wants to take the summit head-on.

Course 2 — Sagimakgol → Summit

📏 12.02km ⛰️ 670m gain ⏱️ 4:07 ➡️ One-way Difficulty ★★★★★ ⤓ GPX

Route: Sagimakgol → Pajujip → the corner shop (Sindo Foods) → Sagimak Park ranger station → restrooms → fork in the trail → no trail toward Sagimakgol → Dulle-gil distance marker → Butimbawi Rock → signpost → valley → national point-location number → lower falls of the Hidden Valley → rest area → lower falls of the Hidden Falls

Getting to the trailhead: Sagimakgol is one of the trailheads where the data actually confirms a bus stop nearby. The trail heads up along the valley past the ranger station and restrooms, and you enter by passing through the village shopping strip.

What it’s like / who it’s for: At 12.02km, this is the longest of the summit routes, so it suits anyone who wants to take their time soaking up the valley and waterfalls along the way. Since it’s a long haul, pacing yourself matters — I’d recommend it for folks who want a leisurely hike with plenty of time to enjoy the sound of running water and the forest.

Course 3 — Fork → Summit

📏 8.38km ⛰️ 607m gain ⏱️ 3:06 ➡️ One-way Difficulty ★★★★★ ⤓ GPX

Via: Fork → Cheongsu Falls → Cheongsu Restroom → Fire-Extinguisher Rest Area → Yeongcheon Spring → Ssangjulgi Spring → Bogungmun Rest Area → Winter Ice-Prone Section → Induam → Hyeongjebong Viewpoint → Dongnyeong Falls → Unnamed Rest Area → Pyeongchang Park Ranger Station Restroom → Pyeongchang Park Ranger Station → Gamnamsan Prayer Retreat

Getting to the trailhead: This course connects toward Pyeongchang and Hyeongjebong. The usual approach is to take a city bus to the residential neighborhood on the downtown side, then walk in from there. Down in the lower section you’ll find the Cheongsu Restroom and the Pyeongchang Park Ranger Station Restroom.

What it’s like / who it’s for: This is a water-rich route, running past Yeongcheon Spring and Ssangjulgi Spring, and you get a nice view from the Hyeongjebong viewpoint. That said, the trail signs specifically flag a “winter ice-prone section,” so you’ll want to be extra careful in the colder months.

Course 4 — Bukhansanseong Ipgu → Baegundae Peak, Bukhansan

📏 9.21km ⛰️ 671m gain ⏱️ 3:25 🔁 Loop Difficulty ★★★★★ ⤓ GPX

Waypoints: Bukhansanseong Ipgu → Bukhansanseong Paid Parking Lot → Information Center → Bukhansanseong Restroom → Junction 5 → Wildflowers → Sugujeong Restroom → Seoamsa Temple → Junction 9 → Restroom by Bukhandong History Museum → Borisa Temple → Gaeyeon Falls → Junction 16 → Daedongsa Spring → Daedongsa Temple

Getting to the trailhead: Bukhansanseong Ipgu is the most iconic starting point on Bukhansan, and it comes well equipped with an information center, a paid parking lot, and restrooms. There’s a good network of city bus routes running right up to the entrance, so getting here by public transit is easy.

What makes it special / who it’s for: This is the classic route that leads straight up to Baegundae Peak, and along the way you’ll pass a bit of everything — fortress walls, temples, valleys, and mountain springs — which makes it a solid pick if it’s your first time on Bukhansan. With restrooms in several spots, it’s also worth recommending to families and visitors from abroad.

Course 5 — Seonunsanjang → Dongjangdae

📏 9.66km ⛰️ 611m gain ⏱️ 3:26 ➡️ One-way Difficulty ★★★★★ ⤓ GPX

Waypoints: Seonunsanjang → Hallelujah Prayer Retreat parking lot → junction → junction rest area → Bukhansan bell rock → Butim Rock rest area → crossing rest area → Doseondawon → Uigwangjang restroom → Baegundae Visitor Support Center → Doseonsa information board → Doseonsa restroom → Yongammun park ranger post → valley rest area → Kim Sanggung stupa (Kim Sanggung Jeonggwanghwa stupa)

Getting to the trailhead: This route links up toward the Ui and Doseonsa (Doseonsa) area, passing the Uigwangjang restroom and the Baegundae Visitor Support Center, which makes it easy to reach. It’s fairly convenient to get to by public transit from the northern part of the city.

Highlights and who it’s for: The trail continues along the fortress-wall ridge toward Dongjangdae (Dongjangdae), with cultural sights like Doseonsa temple and the stupa along the way. With rest areas spaced closely together, it suits anyone who likes to pause often on the climb, or who wants to soak in the fortress-wall views along the ridge.

Course 6 — Fork → Summit

📏 7.83km ⛰️ 602m gain ⏱️ 2:58 🔁 Loop Difficulty ★★★★☆ ⤓ GPX

Route: Fork → Jeongneung Visitor Support Center → Cheongsu-ru → Rest Stop 1 → Rest Stop 2 → Cheongsucheon Spring → Stone Pagoda → Sambongsa Rest Stop → Fitness Area Rest Stop → Yeongchwicheon → Yeongchwisa → Yeongchwisa Five-Story Stone Pagoda → Yeongchwisa Spring → Yeongchwisa Restroom → Seoul City View Point

Trailhead access: This course starts from the visitor support center on the Jeongneung side. It’s close to residential neighborhoods in the city, so it’s easy to reach by local bus. There’s a restroom near the bottom.

Highlights & who it’s for: With a net elevation gain of 602m and a one-way time under 3 hours, this is one of the easier summit-bound routes. There are plenty of water sources along the way — the Cheongsucheon spring, the Yeongchwisa spring, and more — and a Seoul city view point waits at the end, so I’d recommend it to anyone who wants a view over the city.

The highest difficulty in the data is “Moderate,” and the main surfaces are dirt, cement, cement/dirt, and rock/dirt. The dirt and cement paths lower down are easy to walk, but in wet weather the dirt trails get slippery, and the granite rock faces near the summit become very slippery when wet. The data flags a rock/cliff area as a hazard zone, and there are 5 danger-zone POIs recorded along the course (with specific labels like danger warning notices, dangerous footbridges, hazardous trees, and so on), so please be sure to check the signs. There are a generous 36 viewpoints, so you can enjoy the scenery all along the ridge, and there are 5 water sources (spring-water fountains) where you can refill. There are 0 shelters, though, so plan your outing with the understanding that there’s no indoor facility to fall back on at night or in bad weather.

Distance and Time Required

Photo: Korea Tourism Organization photo gallery (Bulgwang-dong, Eunpyeong-gu, Seoul) · KOGL

Bukhansan has no shelters, and its trails run a combined 17.47 km, so whichever route you pick, it’s easily a day hike. The measured summit-area routes range from roughly 2 hours 46 minutes one way (National Point Number → Nahanbong) to about 4 hours 7 minutes (Sagimakgol → summit), so once you factor in the round trip and a few breaks, most of them wrap up in a single day.

For reference, the data lists a cumulative time of 342 minutes going up and 204 minutes coming down across every trail section in the park—but that’s the total from stitching all the sections together, not the time for any one route. That said, the average uphill pace it works out to is about 20 minutes per km, which you can multiply by the distance of your chosen route to get a rough one-way estimate. For a route of around 8 km, for instance, the pace alone puts the climb at roughly 2 hours 30 minutes to 3 hours. Your actual time will vary with your own fitness and any bottlenecks on the rocky ridges near the top.

가는 길(대중교통·주차)

사진: 한국관광공사 관광사진 갤러리 (서울특별시 은평구 불광동) · 공공누리

Bukhansan is one of those mountains that’s genuinely easy to reach on Seoul’s subway and city buses. The usual routine is to get off at a downtown subway station and transfer to a city bus heading for the trailhead. According to the data, the bus stops actually confirmed near the trailheads are Hyoja 2-tong, Sagimakgol, Bukhansan Dongwon Training Ground, and Hyoja Public Safety Center. Sagimakgol in particular drops you right at the start of Course 2.

As for parking, the Korea Forest Service data lists zero registered parking lots in the area, but the signposts on Course 4 do point to the Bukhansanseong paid parking lot. If you’re driving, keep in mind that the paid-parking situation varies from trailhead to trailhead, so it’s worth checking before you head out. For both public transit and parking, I’d recommend confirming the actual routes and real-time directions on KakaoMap or Naver Map (Google Maps can be unreliable for walking and public-transit directions here in Korea).

Season, Weather & Gear

Photo: Korea Tourism Organization Photo Gallery (Bulgwang-dong, Eunpyeong-gu, Seoul) · KOGL

The best seasons are spring (April–May), when the fresh greenery and azaleas are at their finest, and autumn (October–November), when the foliage along the ridges turns beautiful. Summer offers cool valleys, but you’ll want to watch out for heat waves, sudden downpours, and fast-moving water in the streams. In winter, iced-over rock faces near the summit and strong winds crank up the difficulty considerably. Course 3 even has a “chronic winter icing zone” marked right on the trail signs, so take extra care if you’re hiking it in the cold months.

As for essential gear, hiking boots with good grip are a must. There’s a lot of granite slab and stretches with steel railings up top, so you’ll need to be ready for slippery footing. There are water fountains, but depending on the season and conditions they may not be running, so bring plenty of drinking water to be safe. In winter, pack crampons along with windproof, insulating layers; in summer, prepare for UV exposure and dehydration. And since there are so many great viewpoints, a camera and a windbreaker come in handy too.

Tips for International Visitors

Photo: Korea Tourism Organization photo gallery (Bulgwang-dong, Eunpyeong-gu, Seoul) · KOGL

  • Place names in Roman letters: Bukhansan (the mountain) · Baegundae, the main summit (about 834m) · Bukhansanseong Ipgu, the most popular trailhead · Sagimakgol · Doseonsa, on the Ui/Doseonsa side · Jeongneung.
  • Permits and entrance fees: You don’t need any advance permit, and there’s no fee to enter the trails. That said, some trailhead parking lots do charge (like the Bukhansanseong paid parking lot on Course 4).
  • Restrooms and drinking water: You’ll find restrooms at the major trailheads and along the valley routes (Bukhansanseong restroom, Cheongsu restroom, Pyeongchang Park ranger station restroom, Yeongchwisa restroom, and others). The data lists five mountain springs (natural water fountains), but there’s no guarantee they’ll be running at all times, so bring your own water.
  • Map apps: Google Maps can be unreliable for walking and public-transit directions in Korea, so I’d recommend using KakaoMap or Naver Map instead.
  • Safety: The hazardous stretches in the data are the rock-climbing areas, and there are five danger-zone POIs marked. On the summit ridges and in areas prone to rockfall or ice, follow the signs — and since there are no shelters, plan your day so you’re back down before dark. With 36 viewpoints, there are plenty of spots along the ridgelines where you can safely take in the Seoul cityscape.

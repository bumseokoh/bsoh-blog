Ice Valley and the Silver Grass Plateau at Sajapyeong: Jeyaksan in Miryang

Overview

Photo: Korea Tourism Organization photo gallery (Danjang-myeon, Miryang, Gyeongsangnam-do) · Public Nuri

Jaeyaksan is a mountain straddling the Danjang-myeon and Sannae-myeon areas of Miryang in Gyeongsangnam-do, and it forms one of the pillars of the mountain range known as the Yeongnam Alps. Its highest peak reaches 1,211m, and at the foot of the mountain you’ll find Pyochungsa (表忠寺), an ancient temple dating back to the Silla dynasty, along with Miryang Eoreumgol (Ice Valley), which is designated a natural monument — so it’s a place where you can pair your hike with a bit of cultural and natural sightseeing. Overall the mountain has the feel of a forested, earthy peak, but near the summit you’ll find a mix of rocky ridges and lookout boulders. According to the data, the toughest course tops out at a “moderate” difficulty rating, which makes Jaeyaksan a mountain that beginners and intermediate hikers can take on without too much trouble. Most of the routes can be completed as a day hike, but with the trails within the area stretching a combined 67.29km, the terrain is broad enough that if you’re planning a full traverse linking several courses together, it’s worth considering an overnight stay.

Terrain and Ridgelines

Photo: Korea Tourism Organization photo gallery (Danjang-myeon, Miryang-si, Gyeongsangnam-do) · KOGL

Jaeyaksan isn’t a single peak — it’s really a cluster of summits strung together along connecting ridgelines. According to the public peak data, Jaeyaksan Sajabong (1,210m) is the highest point in the area, with Jaeyaksan (1,134m), Jaeyaksan Sumibong (1,137m), Munsubong (965m), and Gwaneumbong (911m) laid out along the surrounding ridges. On top of these, there are several unnamed summit points (646m, 1,124m, and 1,200m) scattered along the trails, which tells you the Jaeyaksan area is densely knit together by ridges and saddles running in every direction. Overall it leans toward being an “earth mountain” — broad slopes of soil and grassland — but the summit ridges mix in viewpoint boulders and rocky stretches, so how the terrain feels underfoot changes depending on which way you climb. The Sajapyeong plateau near the top is especially worth noting: it’s known as one of the rare wide high-altitude wetland and silver-grass meadows in Korea, offering a distinctive kind of scenery you won’t find on the more rock-peak-dominated mountains.

The mountain hike up Jaeyaksan begins in a dense, thickly wooded forest at the trailhead and gradually opens up into ridgeline paths with wider views.

등산의 느낌

사진: 한국관광공사 관광사진 갤러리 (경상남도 밀양시 단장면) · 공공누리

Whether you climb from the Pyochungsa side or the Eoreumgol side, the early stretch follows the valley through deeply shaded forest, and as you gain elevation the trees grow shorter until the ridges of the Yeongnam Alps spread out in every direction. The data bears this out too — seven separate viewpoints are on record — so once you reach the ridge, this is a mountain that rewards you with sweeping views again and again. In autumn, the silver grass across the Sajapyeong plateau near the summit puts on a spectacular show, drawing crowds of hikers, and in summer the valley runs full of water, giving the Eoreumgol course its signature cool, refreshing feel. In winter, parts of the ridge are exposed to strong winds and ice, so there are stretches that carry a real edge of tension — a far cry from the gentle forest path you started on.

Course 1 — Junction 1 → Sajabong Peak, Jaeyaksan

📏 7.52km ⛰️ 1008m gain ⏱️ 3:34 ➡️ One-way Difficulty ★★★★★ ⤓ GPX

Route: Junction 1 → Junction 2 → Junction 3 → Hangyeam Geumgang Falls → Junction 5 → Viewpoint Rock → Sajabong Peak, Jaeyaksan → Junction 8 → Junction 9 → Junction 10 → Junction 15

Getting to the trailhead: This route starts from the Pyochungsa trailhead. Most people get here by taking a Pyochungsa-bound bus from downtown Miryang. There’s parking near the temple grounds too, so driving your own car works as well.

Highlights and who it’s for: You start out walking a forested valley trail past Hangyeam and Geumgang Falls, then in the back half you climb up over Viewpoint Rock to the summit at Sajabong. It’s a course that lets you enjoy both the cool freshness of the valley and the wide-open views up top. Since it targets Sajabong, the highest peak, this is one of the go-to routes for hikers with the summit in mind.

Course 2 — Waypoint 1 → Summit (Long Route)

📏 13.29km ⛰️ 1017m gain ⏱️ 5:01 ➡️ One-way Difficulty ★★★★★ ⤓ GPX

Route: Waypoint 1 → Waypoint 2 → Maebawi Village trail → Waypoints 4–7 → Waypoints 9 & 10 → over Pilbong → Waypoints 12 & 8 → Waypoints 13–15

Getting to the trailhead: This one starts from the trailhead on the Maebawi Village side. It’s an access road into an outlying village, so a car or taxi is a lot more convenient than public transit.

What it’s like / who it’s for: This is the longest course of the bunch. It runs through the Maebawi Village trail and passes several forks along the way, so it’s closer to a full ridge traverse — best suited to experienced hikers who want a long day out. I’d recommend it to anyone with the stamina and the time to spare.

Course 3 — Fork 1 → Summit (Rocky Ridge Views)

📏 6.57km ⛰️ 653m gain ⏱️ 2:44 ➡️ One-way Difficulty ★★★★☆ ⤓ GPX

Via: Forks 1–5 → Forks 16–20 → Forks 22·23 → Viewpoint → Fork 28

Trailhead access: This trailhead starts near Pyochungsa Temple, and since part of the opening stretch is shared with Course 1, the bus and parking situation is much the same.

Highlights & who it’s for: With a net elevation gain of 653m, this one’s relatively gentle, and its standout feature is the way the views open up on the stretch past the viewpoint in the latter half. Since the climb is modest for the distance you cover to the summit, it’s a good fit for hikers who want to take in the scenery without pushing themselves too hard.

Course 4 — Trailhead: Eoreumgol Terminus → Summit

📏 7.63km ⛰️ 845m gain ⏱️ 3:19 ➡️ One-way Difficulty ★★★★★ ⤓ GPX

Route: Eoreumgol Parking Lot 1 → Eoreumgol Management Office → Eoreumgol Parking Lot 2 → Eoreumgol Restroom → Eoreumgol Rest Stop Shop → Eoreumgol Parking Lot 3 → Sumin-ga → Trailhead: Eoreumgol Terminus → Terminus Convenience Store → Bus Timetable → Junction 12 → Guyeon Village Rest Area → Apgujeong Rainbow Pool → Hobakso Falls Signboard → Trailhead

Getting to the trailhead: This course starts from the last stop of the Eoreumgol city bus line in Miryang. Since the Eoreumgol bus route runs all the way to the terminus, it’s about the most accessible option by public transit. Eoreumgol Parking Lots 1 through 3 are laid out one after another, so parking is easy too.

What makes it special / who it’s for: Right at the start you’ll find a management office, restrooms, a shop, and parking lots lined up back to back, making this the course with the richest facilities of the bunch. There’s plenty to see along the way, too — like Hobakso Falls and the Guyeon Village Rest Area — so it’s the course I’d most recommend for first-time visitors to Jaeyaksan or for hikers who value having conveniences close at hand.

Course 5 — Viewpoint → Summit

📏 6.96km ⛰️ 869m gain ⏱️ 3:11 🔁 Loop Difficulty ★★★★★ ⤓ GPX

Passing through: Viewpoint → Fork 10 → Jinbularam Viewpoint → Forks 24·25 → Jinbularam → Forks 27·28

Trailhead access: This is the trailhead on the Jinbularam side, and most people head in through the grounds of Pyochungsa Temple before joining the mountain path.

What makes it special / who it’s for: True to its name, the course starts at a viewpoint and passes the Jinbularam Viewpoint again along the way, so you get these scenic stretches coming back around more than once. You can also take in Jinbularam, an old temple, while you’re there, which makes it a good fit for anyone who wants to combine a temple visit with their hike.

Course 6 — First Saddle on the Climb → Summit

📏 7.88km ⛰️ 777m gain ⏱️ 3:16 ➡️ One-way Difficulty ★★★★★ ⤓ GPX

Passes through: First saddle on the climb → Junctions 10·16 → Wetland Protection Area → Viewpoint → Sijapyeong-gil shade shelter → Junctions 35·36·40·50·52·57 → Junctions 103·106

Getting to the trailhead: This is a trailhead heading toward Sajapyeong, and since it runs through the Wetland Protection Area, most people approach from either Pyochungsa Temple or the Sannae-myeon side.

Highlights & who it’s for: This is the only course that passes straight through the Wetland Protection Area, so it gets you closest to the silver grass meadows of the Sajapyeong plateau. It’s an especially good pick for hikers coming out to see the silver grass in autumn.

※ The figures above are the total trail lengths tallied by administrative district — they aren’t the one-way distance of any single course.

The surface details aren’t published down to the specific material, but given the net elevation gain near the summit (653m–1017m) and the measured difficulty (★★★★☆–★★★★★), you can expect the grade to steepen the closer you get to the top, and you’ll want to watch your footing along the rocky ridges and lookout boulders. The data flags one hazardous spot, so it’s worth slowing down and putting safety first through that stretch. There are seven identified viewpoints, so the ridge walk opens up to sweeping views more than once, and there are eight points used as trailheads or exit points. That said, no shelters show up in the public data, so keep in mind there’s nowhere to take cover from rain or rest if the weather turns suddenly — it’s safer to give yourself plenty of time in your plans. In winter, watch for ice along the ridgeline, and in summer, be mindful of streams swelling quickly after rain.

거리와 소요시간

사진: 한국관광공사 관광사진 갤러리 (경상남도 밀양시 단장면) · 공공누리

The one-way distances for the summit-area courses range from 5.17km to 13.29km — a big spread depending on which route you take — and the estimated times vary just as much, from about 2 hours 34 minutes to 5 hours 1 minute one way. Most courses take somewhere in the range of 5 to 10 hours round trip, so you can comfortably knock one out as a day hike. That said, if you’re planning to walk the whole thing end to end — the full 67.29km of trails in the area strung together — the tally comes to 1,265 minutes (about 21 hours) of cumulative climbing on the way up and 878 minutes (about 14.6 hours) on the way down. It’s worth being clear that this is a running total across every section in the area, not the time for any single course. Based on that cumulative figure, the average uphill pace works out to about 19 minutes per km, which is a handy reference when you’re trying to ballpark how long a single summit course might take. Keep in mind that the longer courses with more elevation gain will likely run a bit slower than that pace, so if it’s your first time out, it’s a good idea to pad the estimated times in the table with some breathing room when you plan.

가는 길(대중교통·주차)

사진: 한국관광공사 관광사진 갤러리 (경상남도 밀양시 단장면) · 공공누리

Since Jeyaksan sits in Miryang, Gyeongsangnam-do, there’s no subway option — the usual approach is to catch an intercity or local bus from downtown Miryang heading toward Pyochungsa Temple or Eoreumgol. If you’re coming from a nearby city like Busan or Daegu, a popular route is to take a KTX or Mugunghwa train to Miryang Station, then transfer to a bus bound for Pyochungsa or Eoreumgol. According to public data, the bus stops near the trailheads include Juam Village Entrance, Pyochungsa Shopping Area, and Sijeon, and from any of these you can walk to the start of your chosen course. If you’re driving, there’s parking near Pyochungsa and at the Eoreumgol Parking Lots 1 through 3, though public data lists only one lot, so expect crowds on weekends and during peak season. For accurate routes and bus timetables, your best bet is to search “Pyochungsa” or “Miryang Eoreumgol” in KakaoMap or Naver Map and check the public transit directions there.

Season, Weather & Gear

Photo: Korea Tourism Organization photo gallery (Danjang-myeon, Miryang, Gyeongsangnam-do) · KOGL

Jaeyaksan is generally considered best for hiking in spring and fall, and autumn in particular draws crowds who come to see the silver grass fields at Sajapyeong near the summit. In summer, the valleys run full and you can enjoy the cool, refreshing air—especially along the Eolmugol (Ice Valley) course—but you’ll want to be ready for the heat, humidity, and sudden rain. Winter can bring strong winds along the ridgeline and icy stretches, so crampons and windproof gear become necessary. In any season, it’s a good idea to pack the basics: hiking boots, plenty of drinking water (there are almost no springs or water fountains along the way, so I’d recommend carrying at least 1–2 liters), a rain jacket or windbreaker, and trekking poles.

Foreigner Travel Tips

Photo: Korea Tourism Organization Photo Gallery (Danjang-myeon, Miryang-si, Gyeongsangnam-do) · KOGL

Jaeyaksan is written in the Roman alphabet as Jaeyaksan, and its main peak, Sajabong, can be rendered as Jaeyaksan Sajabong. Nearby, Pyochungsa is usually signposted as Pyochungsa Temple, and Eoreumgol as Miryang Ice Valley (Eoreumgol). There’s no separate hiking permit or entrance fee for the mountain itself, but if you go inside the Pyochungsa temple grounds, a temple admission fee may apply, so it’s worth checking before you visit. Restrooms are available at facilities near the trailheads, such as the Eoreumgol restroom on the Ice Valley route, but there are almost no toilets or drinking fountains along the trail itself, so make sure to bring plenty of water before you set off. As for map apps, Google Maps often doesn’t accurately reflect Korea’s hiking trails, so I’d recommend using KakaoMap or Naver Map instead. For safety, give yourself plenty of time for the descent, don’t rush through sections marked as dangerous, and since there may be spots with poor cell reception, it’s a good idea to save the trail map offline beforehand.

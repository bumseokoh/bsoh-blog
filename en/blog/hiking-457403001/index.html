개요

Photo: Korea Tourism Organization photo gallery (Bugye-myeon, Gunwi-gun, Daegu) · KOGL

Palgongsan is one of the best-known mountains just outside Daegu, spreading across Dong-gu at its heart and reaching into Gunwi-gun, Yeongcheon-si, and Gyeongsan-si. Based on survey data, the highest point around the summit sits at roughly 1,204m, though the peak listings put Birobong—effectively the main summit—at 1,220m, so the figures vary a bit from source to source. It’s a mountain that mixes the gentle slopes of an earthen mountain with rugged granite ridgelines. The main routes up to the summit area run about 4.5–8.7km one way and take anywhere from 2 hours 15 minutes to 3 hours 20 minutes, so for most people a single day is plenty. The difficulty varies a lot depending on which course you pick—everything from mellow, pilgrimage-like stretches to advanced routes strung together with rocky ridges and cliffs.

Terrain and mountain form

Photo: Korea Tourism Organization photo gallery (Bugye-myeon, Gunwi-gun, Daegu) · KOGL

Palgongsan is essentially an earthen mountain, but its ridgelines are studded with granite crags and rocky stretches. According to the peak data, the main summit, Birobong (1,220m), anchors a ridge that runs on to Dongbong (1,193–1,194m, including the 1,189m figure given for Palgongsan’s Dongbong) and Seobong, while other peaks like Yeombulbong (1,133m), Sillimbong (828m), and Geojeosan (502m) round out the range. The waypoint data for the summit-area courses is full of place names pointing to rocky terrain — Naktabong (Camel Peak), oddly shaped boulders, cliff sections — which shows just how many exposed rock ridges have formed along the crest. At the foot of Palgongsan sit venerable Buddhist sites such as Donghwasa Temple and Gatbawi (Gwanbong), and the mountain is widely known for how tightly its landscape is bound up with religious and historical scenery.

The Feel of the Hike

Photo: Korea Tourism Organization photo gallery (Bugye-myeon, Gunwi-gun, Daegu) · KOGL

How Palgongsan feels really depends on which course you take. The lower stretches near temples like Donghwasa are thick with pine and broadleaf forest — gentle, heavily shaded paths. As you get closer to the ridgeline, granite outcrops and lookout points start alternating, and the views open up. Sections built around contemplation and walking, like the Wonhyo Guido Trail, come with foot-washing spots, pavilions, and signboards inscribed with old sayings, so they feel less like a climb and more like a stroll. Courses that include the steep “gasping slope” (kkalttak-gogae) or rock faces, on the other hand, pitch up sharply and are dotted with repeated warnings about falling rocks and cliffs, making for a more nerve-wracking hike. As for the seasons, Palgongsan is known for fresh spring greenery and azaleas, the sound of water in the valleys come summer, ridgeline foliage in autumn (it’s well known as one of Daegu’s signature spots for fall colors), and snow and hoarfrost along the ridges in winter.

Course 1 — Bus Stop → Kkalttak Pass Summit Rest Area

📏 8.67km ⛰️ 721m gain ⏱️ 3:22 ➡️ One-way Difficulty ★★★★★ ⤓ GPX

Route: Bus stop → fork in the trail → restroom → Kkalttak Pass summit rest area → rock formation → rockfall danger zone → clifftop viewpoint → Sillimjeong pavilion → Nakta Peak (Camel Peak) → rockfall danger zone → viewpoint → path to the Seated Rock-carved Bhaisajyaguru Buddha → Palgongsan Seated Rock-carved Bhaisajyaguru Buddha → tree growing from a rock crevice → viewpoint atop the deck stairs

Getting to the trailhead: The trailhead sits right next to the bus stop, so this is a transit-dependent course. Several Daegu city bus routes run toward Palgongsan, so I’d recommend checking the exact stop and route on KakaoMap or Naver Map.

Highlights and who it’s for: This course passes Buddhist cultural heritage sites like Nakta Peak and the Palgongsan Seated Rock-carved Bhaisajyaguru Buddha. You’ll see two rockfall danger zone signs along the way, so be especially careful of falling rocks after rain. As the name “Kkalttak Pass” (roughly, “gasping-for-breath pass”) suggests, the steep sections stretch on for a good while, making this one better suited to hikers who are confident in their fitness.

Course 2 — Wonhyo Gudo Trail Entrance → Sky Garden Overlook

📏 7.08km ⛰️ 621m gain ⏱️ 2:48 🔁 Loop Difficulty ★★★★☆ ⤓ GPX

Waypoints: Restroom → Wonhyo Gudo Trail Information Board → Junction 4 → Wonhyo Gudo Trail Entrance → Rest Area → Signpost → Foot-Washing Station → You-Are-Here Marker → Inscription → Cheongunjeong Pavilion → Viewpoint → Portable Restroom → “Standing on the Path Wonhyo Once Walked” → Junction 17 → Odoam Entrance

Getting to the trailhead: The Wonhyo Gudo Trail is a pilgrimage-style walking path laid out on the northern skirts of Palgongsan, and from what I’ve read, most people get there by car rather than public transit. It’s worth checking the exact access road and parking situation on a map app before you go.

What it’s like / who it’s for: With contemplative touches scattered all along the way — the foot-washing station, Cheongunjeong Pavilion, the inscription boards — it feels less like a pure climb and more like a “walking pilgrimage.” The view from the Sky Garden Overlook is the real highlight, and since it’s a gentle, mostly dirt path, it works well for families and first-time trekkers too.

Course 3 — Donghwasa Entrance Parking Lot → Summit

📏 6.54km ⛰️ 693m gain ⏱️ 2:47 🔁 Loop Difficulty ★★★★☆ ⤓ GPX

Via: Parking lot → Restrooms → Rest area → Kkalttak Pass → Scenic viewpoint → You-are-here signpost → Fork → Donghwasa Entrance Parking Lot → Snack shop·Parking lot → Tourist attraction → Donghwaji Reservoir

Getting to the trailhead: You start out from the entrance to Donghwasa, one of the flagship temples on Palgongsan. The Daegu metro doesn’t reach this stretch, so most people take a Daegu city bus, and with the Donghwasa Entrance Parking Lot right there, this is one of the easier courses to get to by car.

What it’s like·who it’s for: The conveniences — Donghwaji Reservoir along with the snack shop and parking lot — are all clustered right at the start, so it’s an easy one to access, and it’s a classic “temple base → summit” course where the grade kicks up sharply along the Kkalttak Pass stretch. A good pick if it’s your first time on Palgongsan.

Course 4 — Trailhead → Mangpokdae Lookout

📏 7.94km ⛰️ 789m gain ⏱️ 3:18 ➡️ One-way Difficulty ★★★★★ ⤓ GPX

Via: Restroom → Parking lot → Trailhead → Palgongsan Chisan District Visitor Center → Fork 8 → Trail sign → Chisan Valley → Clearing → Fork 15 → Mangpokdae Lookout → Poem inside Mangpokdae → Chisan Falls → Trail entrance → Fork 23 → Fork 25

Getting to the trailhead: The starting point is the Chisan District Visitor Center over on the Yeongcheon side. It’s a fair distance from central Daegu, so getting there by car is your best bet. There’s reportedly parking available near the visitor center.

What it’s like / who it’s for: This course follows Chisan Valley on the way up, and the valley scenery really stands out — Chisan Falls, Mangpokdae Lookout, and more. It’s a popular summer valley hike, but keep in mind that rain can bring fast currents and rising water, so take care in wet weather. A good fit for anyone who loves trekking along a valley.

Course 5 — Rest Area → The Spot Where You Can See the Summit

📏 8.5km ⛰️ 704m gain ⏱️ 3:18 ➡️ One-way Difficulty ★★★★★ ⤓ GPX

Via: Rest area → Suneungbongsan-gye marker → Restroom → Rock face → Rock-climbing accident danger zone → Fork 14 → Waterfall → Cliff hazard warning → Old-tree bench → Fork 18 → The spot where you can see the summit → The seat with a summit view → Palgongsan ridge → Village scenery → Strange rock formations

Getting to the trailhead: This is a stretch where historical markers like the Suneungbongsan-gye stone still remain, so check a map app for exact public transit and parking details before you go.

Highlights & who it’s for: With warning signs like “rock-climbing accident danger zone” and “cliff hazard warning” coming one after another, this is an advanced-level course with a heavy share of rocky ridge and cliff sections. But the payoff is just as big — the strange rock formations and the views out over the Palgongsan ridge.

Course 6 — From Birobong toward Odojae → Seonginbong

📏 8.31km ⛰️ 686m gain ⏱️ 3:13 ➡️ One-way Difficulty ★★★★★ ⤓ GPX

Route: From Birobong toward Odojae → Giwa → Seonginbong

Getting to the trailhead: This section has a strong through-hike feel, following the ridgeline near Birobong, Palgongsan’s main peak. So rather than starting from its own trailhead, most people reach it by linking it up with another course. You’ll want to check transport and parking details separately.

Highlights and who it’s for: There isn’t much information on the waypoints, but the place name “Giwa” (meaning roof tile) hints at the remains of an old building site. It’s a nice section to fold into a through-hike connecting Birobong and Seonginbong.

Difficulty, terrain, and cautions

Photo: Korea Tourism Organization photo gallery (Bugye-myeon, Gunwi-gun, Daegu) · KOGL

The local trail spatial data has four sections registered by administrative district, and the highest difficulty rating in the data is “Easy.” There’s no separate information on the trail surface.

That “Easy” label applies to the low-lying sections registered locally, which are a different beast from the courses that climb to the summit area. In fact, my GPX measurements of the Top 100 Mountains courses show that most of the summit-bound routes have a net elevation gain in the 600–800m range, putting them at a difficulty of ★★★★☆ to ★★★★★ — so they feel quite a bit tougher than the rating suggests.

As for facilities, the trail spatial data lists zero POIs in every category — water fountains, shelters, hazard zones, viewpoints, trailheads, and trail exits — so you should basically assume there are no officially registered facilities. That said, the actual GPX-measured waypoints on the summit courses do turn up on-site hazard signs: several restrooms, two rockfall-risk zones (Course 1), and a rock-climbing accident-risk zone and cliff-hazard caution stretch (Course 5). So keep in mind there’s a gap between the registered facility stats and the signs you’ll actually find on the ground. The rocky sections get very slippery when iced over, and right after rain you’ll want to be especially careful around the areas marked for rockfall.

Distance and Time Required

Photo: Korea Tourism Organization photo gallery (Bugye-myeon, Gunwi-gun, Daegu) · KOGL

The 6.76km total trail length within the area is the sum of all four sections I mentioned earlier — it’s not the distance of any single course. Against that total, the recorded times are 120 minutes going up and 84 minutes coming down, which works out to an average ascent pace of about 18 minutes per km. This figure, too, is a cumulative total across every section in the area, so don’t mistake it for the time of any one particular course.

What’s actually worth referencing for reaching the summit is the GPX-based estimated times from the Korea’s 100 Famous Mountains data, where the main courses fall somewhere between roughly 2 hours 15 minutes and 3 hours 22 minutes one way. Unless you’re doing a round trip or a full traverse, most of these are perfectly doable as a day hike, and since there’s no registered shelter, I’d recommend going as a day trip rather than planning an overnight hike. That said, if you’re linking several courses together for a traverse, the total time can exceed 6 hours, so it’s a good idea to give yourself some breathing room — setting out right after sunrise, for instance.

The Route There (Public Transit & Parking)

Photo: Korea Tourism Organization photo gallery (Bugye-myeon, Gunwi-gun, Daegu) · KOGL

Daegu’s urban rail doesn’t run all the way out to Palgongsan, so getting there mostly means hopping on a Daegu city bus. The one stop I could actually confirm near the trailhead is “Donghwasa Ipgu-ap” (Donghwasa Entrance), so if you’re taking a Donghwasa-side course, plan around that stop. The Gatbawi (Gwanbong) side and the Pagyesa/Sutaegol side each connect via different bus routes, so for the stop and transfer details that match your particular course, definitely check the real-time public transit directions on KakaoMap or Naver Map.

As for parking, the trail’s official spatial data lists zero registered parking facilities. In practice, though, there’s a “Donghwasa Entrance Parking Lot” along Course 3 and some parking space near the Chisan District visitor center along Course 4. In other words, there’s informal parking at the entrances to the main temples and visitor centers, but none of it is officially registered as trail-affiliated parking — so on weekends and peak season, factor in the likelihood of parking congestion.

시즌·날씨·장비

사진: 한국관광공사 관광사진 갤러리 (경상북도 경산시 와촌면) · 공공누리

For hiking Palgongsan, spring (April–May) and fall (October–November) are widely considered the most comfortable seasons in terms of temperature and views. Summer has its own appeal — you can combine a hike with a dip in one of the valley courses like Chisan Valley — but you’ll want to watch out for humidity and thunderstorms. In winter, ice and hoarfrost frequently form on the rocky ridgelines, which pushes the perceived difficulty up considerably.

As for must-have gear: ankle-supporting hiking boots, plenty of drinking water (figure at least 1–1.5L per person, since there are basically no water fountains on the trails), and rain gear plus a spare set of clothes for wet weather or rockfall zones. In winter, be sure to pack crampons, gaiters, and warm gloves. For courses with lots of rocky ridges, I’d also recommend gloves and a headlamp (in case you end up descending later than planned), and for summer trekking, sunscreen and insect repellent both come in handy.

Tips for International Travelers

Photo: Korea Tourism Organization Photo Gallery (Wachon-myeon, Gyeongsan-si, Gyeongsangbuk-do) · KOGL

Palgongsan is romanized as “Palgongsan,” its main peak is “Birobong,” and its landmark temple is “Donghwasa.” There’s no record of any special entry permit or trail admission fee for the mountain as a whole, though when you visit temple grounds you should follow the on-site guidance. The count of registered drinking fountains comes to zero, so be sure to bring your own water before setting out. Restrooms are available at the trailheads and midway points of several courses (near parking lots and rest areas), so it’s a good idea to use them before you start hiking.

As for map apps, Google Maps is often unreliable for walking and public transit directions within Korea, so be sure to install KakaoMap or Naver Map to check real-time routes and bus stop information. For your safety, follow the signage in sections marked for falling-rock or cliff hazards, keep your phone battery well charged, and in an emergency call 119 (emergency services). In particular, courses like Course 1 and Course 5, where falling-rock and cliff warning signs appear repeatedly, are best suited to those with plenty of climbing experience.

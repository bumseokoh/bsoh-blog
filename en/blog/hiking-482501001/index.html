개요

Photo: Korea Tourism Organization photo gallery (Yangju-si, Gyeonggi-do) · KOGL

Dobongsan (道峰山) sits mostly within Seoul’s Dobong-gu but spills over into Uijeongbu-si and Yangju-si in Gyeonggi-do, and together with Bukhansan it forms one pillar of Bukhansan National Park. The highest peak is usually said to be Jaunbong, at roughly 740m or so, but the actual trails lead hikers up to Sinseondae — a summit-area point at 755m — while the highest elevation in the peak zone reaches 759m. With its prominent granite crags, it’s a “bone mountain” (golsan), and even among the famous peaks near Seoul it stands out for its rocky ridgelines. According to the local trail spatial data, the toughest grade on record here comes in as “easy,” so beginners can tackle the shorter sections without much trouble, and most of the courses aiming for the peak zone — Sinseondae, Yeoseongbong, Obong and the like — are easily done as a day hike.

Terrain and Ridgelines

Photo: Korea Tourism Organization photo gallery (Yangju, Gyeonggi-do) · KOGL

Dobongsan is a classic rock mountain — granite peaks and ridges packed tightly one after another. Its signature summits include Sinseondae (755m, also known as the Dobongsan Sinseondae) near the top and the neighboring Peak 656 (660m), the old fire-watch post on Podae Ridge (666m), Yeoseongbong Peak (505m) on the western ridge, and Obong (671m), the cluster of strange rock formations that’s practically the mountain’s emblem. Along the descent you’ll also find lower viewpoints, like the Twin Observatory (152m). Massive, near-vertical granite pillars such as Seoninbong and Manjangbong rise from the valleys here and there, which has earned the mountain a reputation for rock climbing, and the ridge sections string together well-known rocks like Madang Rock and Face Rock. The valleys — Wondobong, Dobong, Songchu and others — are gentle enough that they draw summer waders, but the closer you get to the ridge, the more the steep rock slopes come one after another, giving the mountain its sharply varied character.

The Feel of the Hike

Photo: Korea Tourism Organization Photo Gallery (Yangju, Gyeonggi-do) · KOGL

The lower stretch mostly winds gently along broadleaf forest and a stream, but as you near the ridge, granite slabs and rock staircases come one after another and the perceived difficulty shoots up — that’s the signature rhythm of Dobongsan. Viewpoints and photo spots framed against Seoninbong and Manjangbong are scattered throughout the course, adding to the fun of the walk, and once you reach the summit area, a wide-open view that takes in downtown Seoul and even the ridgeline of Bukhansan opens up like a reward. The fresh greenery and azaleas at the mouth of the valley are especially lovely in spring, the autumn foliage set against the rocky ridges in fall, and in winter, ice lingers on the shaded rock sections — so even on the same course, the difficulty you feel can vary quite a bit with the season.

Course 1 — Fork → Dobongsan Sinseondae

📏 3.75km ⛰️ 629m gain ⏱️ 1:59 ➡️ One-way Difficulty ★★★☆☆ ⤓ GPX

Route: Fork → Rest area → Sinseondae viewpoint → Photo spot → Overlook → Dobongsan Sinseondae → Seonin Safety Rest area → Rockfall hazard → Watch for wild boars → Madangbawi Rest area → Madangbawi → Small rest area → Portable toilet

Getting to the trailhead: It’s widely known that the trail starts near the Dobongsan Visitor Support Center, over toward Dobongsan Station (Subway Line 1). Since it’s pretty easy to reach by public transit, it’s counted as one of Dobongsan’s signature courses.

What it’s like and who it’s for: The Sinseondae viewpoint and photo spots come one after another, so there are plenty of places to snap pictures the whole way up, and the view really opens up in the Madangbawi section right before the summit. That said, there are signs warning of rockfall hazards and wild boars, so if you’re hiking alone you’ll want to stick to the safety basics. It’s a good fit for first-time visitors to Dobongsan, or anyone who wants a quick taste of the summit views.

Course 2 — Injeolmi Rock → Y-Valley Summit

📏 5.13km ⛰️ 646m gain ⏱️ 2:22 ➡️ One-way Difficulty ★★★★☆ ⤓ GPX

Route: Injeolmi Rock → fork in the trail → Seoninbong viewpoint → Seoninbong·Manjangbong backdrop photo zone → Manwolam Temple 2 → Manwolam Temple 1 → Manwolam Mountain Spirit Shrine → Seonmanja photo zone → stair rest area → falling hazard → Y-Valley summit → chronic winter icing section → Seokgulam → Pureunsaem (Blue Spring) → three-way junction safety rest area

Getting to the trailhead: This one’s known as the route that heads toward Manwolam Temple from near Dobongsan Station, and it’s a stretch shared by temple visitors and hikers alike.

What stands out / who it’s for: This course lets you take in the massive rock faces of Seoninbong and Manjangbong up close, and the ridge scenery here is about as dramatic as it gets. That said, the Y-Valley section is clearly marked with falling-hazard signs and a chronic winter icing zone, so you’ll want to detour in rain or icy conditions — I’d really only recommend it to folks with some rocky-ridge hiking under their belt.

Course 3 — Dobongsan Daewonsa Temple (大圓寺) → Wondobong 1 Safety Rest Area

📏 9.3km ⛰️ 669m gain ⏱️ 3:26 ➡️ One-way Difficulty ★★★★★ ⤓ GPX

Waypoints: Dobongsan Daewonsa Temple (大圓寺) → Bukhansan National Park Wondobong Office Volunteer Center → Yaksan Garden → Wondobong Parking Lot 3 → Wondobong Restroom → Wondobong Parking Lot 1 → Wondobong Rest Area → Trail Fork → Wondobong Parking Lot 2 → Ssangnyongsa Temple (雙龍寺) → Restroom below Jijangam → Wonhyosa Temple (元孝寺) → Myobeop Yeonhwagyeong → Anmalsaem Spring → Anmal Safety Rest Area

Getting to the trailhead: You enter from the Wondobong side near Mangwolsa or Hoeryong Station (Subway Line 1). Since Wondobong Parking Lots 1 through 3 run one after another, this is a stretch that’s relatively easy to reach by car as well.

What it’s like / who it’s for: With temples like Daewonsa, Ssangnyongsa, and Wonhyosa lined up one after another, this trail lets you combine a walk along the valley with a bit of temple-hopping, and the opening section stays nice and gentle. At 9.3 km it’s the longest of the bunch, and restrooms and rest areas are spaced closely all along the way — so it’s a good fit for anyone who wants to take their time and stroll at a leisurely pace.

Course 4 — Temple Parking Lot → Summit

📏 5.86km ⛰️ 648m gain ⏱️ 2:33 ➡️ One-way Difficulty ★★★★☆ ⤓ GPX

Via: Temple parking lot → trail fork → Seoninbong photo spot → Seoninbong overlook rest area → rest area below Cheonchuksa → Cheonchuksa Falls → Cheonchuksa main gate rest area → gujeolcho and yellow cosmos fields → Cheonchuksa spring → Cheonchuksa Temple → Madangbawi spring → first-aid box rest area → log-bench rest area → Gwaneum Falls → Face Rock

Getting to the trailhead: This course starts from the temple parking lot on the Cheonchuksa access road, over toward Dobongsan Station. It’s known as a stretch that sees plenty of visitors, whether they walk in or grab one of the handful of parking spots.

What makes it special / who it’s for: There’s a lot to see through the seasons around the temple grounds — Cheonchuksa Falls, the main gate, the fields of gujeolcho and cosmos — and with two marked springs (the Cheonchuksa spring and the Madangbawi spring), topping up your water is fairly easy. It’s a good fit for anyone who wants to take in both the temple scenery and the rocky-ridge views.

Course 5 — Trailhead → Obong Summit Viewpoint

📏 12.65km ⛰️ 580m gain ⏱️ 4:08 🔁 Loop Difficulty ★★★★★ ⤓ GPX

Route: gentle valley → mountain trail and stream → striking rock formations → small tributary → Songchu Falls → midway up Songchu Falls → top of Songchu Falls → rest area → valley viewpoint → rocky rest spot → natural rest area → trail fork → Obong summit viewpoint → Obong viewpoint → caution: steep drop

Getting to the trailhead: This course starts from the entrance on the Songchu Valley side. Rather than heading to Dobongsan Station, the usual approach is to take a local bus toward Songchu.

What it’s like & who it’s for: At 12.65km, this is the longest of the eight courses. The early stretch follows the valley alongside Songchu Falls, so it’s a lovely walk in summer with the sound of running water. The highlight is the spot where you get a head-on view of Obong, but there’s a steep drop-off section in the latter half, so it’s best suited to fit hikers who are up for a full traverse.

Course 6 — Wang Gomjangeo → Summit

📏 7.68km ⛰️ 611m gain ⏱️ 2:56 ➡️ One-way Difficulty ★★★★☆ ⤓ GPX

Waypoints: Wang Gomjangeo → Sangol Boribap → Howon Police Substation Owl Post → Protected Tree → Ginkgo Tree Spring → Hoeryong Visitor Support Center → Hoeryong Visitor Support Center Restroom → Roadside Parking → Fitness Area Rest Stop → Fork → Hoeryong Spring → Hoeryong Falls → Hoeryongsa 3 → Hoeryongsa 2 → Hoeryongsa 1

Getting to the trailhead: You enter from the Howon-dong side, near Hoeryong Station on subway Line 1. The Hoeryong Visitor Support Center has both a restroom and roadside parking, so this stretch is relatively well set up with conveniences right at the start.

Highlights and who it’s for: There are several water-related spots marked along the way — the Ginkgo Tree Spring, Hoeryong Spring, Hoeryong Falls — so this route has a much stronger valley atmosphere than the others, plus a bit of cultural flavor as it passes three temple sites around Hoeryongsa. Since you can reach it straight from Hoeryong Station, it’s an easy choice for anyone who prefers to get to their hikes by public transit.

난이도·노면·주의

사진: 한국관광공사 관광사진 갤러리 (경기도 양주시) · 공공누리

According to the local trail spatial data, there are two registered sections. The Jeongchak Valley Reservoir–beside Daegammyeon Office section (3.08km, easy, rock/soil surface) is flagged with a hazard marker for steep grades and rocky terrain, while the Mukbang-ri section (0.63km, easy, rock/soil surface) has no separate hazard marking. Both sections have rock/soil surfaces, so you’ll want to watch your footing on the bare-rock stretches even in dry weather — and it feels a lot more slippery in the rain or in fall once the leaves pile up. In the trail data, the number of registered water fountains, shelters, viewpoints, and hazard zones is zero for each, while trailheads and exit points come to three. That said, the actual surveyed GPX route does mark spots by name for falling-rock danger, wild boar sightings, fall/drop danger, and a stretch that ices over every winter (Y Valley), so when you pass through those areas, be sure to check the signs — and above all, don’t head into the icy winter section without crampons.

Distance and Time

Photo: Korea Tourism Organization photo gallery (Yangju, Gyeonggi-do) · KOGL

The total length of the hiking trails within the district (adding up the Jeongchak Valley Reservoir–Daegam-myeon Office section and the Mukbang-ri section) comes to 3.71 km — but that’s just the combined total of several sections, not the distance of any single course. Across all the sections in the district, the cumulative time works out to 67 minutes going up and 46 minutes coming down, which converts to an average uphill pace of about 18 minutes per kilometer. Think of that pace as a rough reference for estimating how long a given course might take — don’t mistake the 67 minutes up and 46 minutes down for the time of one single course. Based on actual GPX measurements, one-way times on the summit-area courses range from as little as about two hours (Sinseondae, Yeoseongbong) to more than four hours at the longer end (the Obong summit lookout, the Daewonsa course), and a round trip or full traverse can run six to eight hours or more. That said, whichever course you pick, there’s plenty of time for a round trip in a single day — and since there are no shelters up there, it’s safest to plan on finishing your descent before the sun goes down.

Getting There (Public Transit & Parking)

Photo: Korea Tourism Organization photo gallery (Yangju, Gyeonggi-do) · KOGL

Dobongsan is wonderfully easy to reach from central Seoul, since Subway Line 1 wraps around the base of the mountain with stops at Dobongsan, Mangwolsa, and Hoeryong stations. From Dobongsan Station you can walk straight onto the trails heading toward Sinseondae, Cheonchuksa, and the Y Valley, while Mangwolsa and Hoeryong stations put you at the start of the Wondobong and Hoeryongsa routes. The Songchu side (the course with views of the Obong peaks) requires a separate city bus, so it’s worth checking the route ahead of time. Since the area’s trail mapping data doesn’t list any registered bus stops or parking lots, you’re better off leaning on a subway-and-bus combination rather than driving. If you do decide to bring a car, it’s a good idea to check apps like KakaoMap or Naver Map in advance — along with the actual route — to see whether there’s small-scale parking near your course, such as the Wondobong Parking Lots No. 1–3 or a temple’s own lot. Both apps offer transit transfer info and real-time trail guidance, which makes them handy not just for locals but for international travelers too.

시즌·날씨·장비

Photo: Korea Tourism Organization Photo Gallery (Dobong-dong, Dobong-gu, Seoul) · KOGL

The best times to hike are spring (April–May), when fresh greenery comes in and the azaleas bloom, and autumn (October–November), when the fall colors are at their peak. In summer you can combine your hike with a dip in the valley streams, but during the monsoon season the rock faces get very slippery in the rain, so waterproof hiking boots and poles are a must. In winter, ice often lingers on shaded rocky ridges like Y Valley, so be sure to pack crampons (or gaiters). During the icy months, beginners are safer sticking to gently sloping valley trails rather than ridge routes like Sinseondae or Y Valley. As for the gear everyone should have, I’d point to hiking boots that support your ankles, trekking poles, a spare set of clothes, and plenty of drinking water.

Tips for International Travelers

Photo: Korea Tourism Organization photo gallery (Dobong-dong, Dobong-gu, Seoul) · KOGL

Dobongsan is romanized as Dobongsan, its near-summit peak Sinseondae as Sinseondae, and the other peaks as Obong and Yeoseongbong. Dobongsan sits within Bukhansan National Park, and you’re free to hike it without any entry permit or admission fee. There are restrooms scattered along the trails — portable toilets, the Wondobong restroom, the one below Jijangam, and the one at the Hoeryong Visitor Support Center — but according to the local trail data, there don’t appear to be any officially registered drinking fountains. So rather than counting on natural springs like the Ginkgo Tree spring, the springs at Cheoncheuksa or Madangbawi, or the Hoeryong spring, it’s safer to bring at least 1 to 1.5 liters of water with you. As for map apps, Google Maps often fails to show Korean trail information accurately, so I’d recommend installing KakaoMap or Naver Map to check the actual routes and public transit. On the safety front, it’s worth remembering to take detours around rocky ridge sections marked with Y-Valley, fall-risk, or rockfall-risk signs when it’s raining or icy — and that in an emergency you can call 119, Korea’s unified rescue hotline.

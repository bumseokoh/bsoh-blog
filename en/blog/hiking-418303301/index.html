Yumyeongsan: Where Valley Streams Meet Rocky Ridgelines

Overview

Photo: Korea Tourism Organization photo gallery (Uljin-gun, Gyeongsangbuk-do) · KOGL Type 1

Yumyeongsan straddles the border between Gapyeong-gun and Yangpyeong-gun in Gyeonggi-do. Its summit elevation is listed slightly differently depending on the source — 869m, 883m, 884m — though trail surveys put the actual high point at 889m. It’s part of a ridgeline that connects to the neighboring peaks of Eobisan, Sogunisan, and Jungmisan, and while the mountain is mostly soil-covered (yuksan-style), there are some rocky stretches along the ridge. Trail data rates it as no harder than “easy” at its gentlest, so there are routes beginners can handle — but depending on which trailhead you start from, you can just as easily end up on a long, demanding hike of over four hours one way. Either way, it’s a solid day-hike option, and in summer it’s also well known as a spot for cooling off in the valley streams.

Mountain Shape and Terrain

Photo: Korea Tourism Organization photo gallery (Uljin County, Gyeongsangbuk-do) · KOGL Type 1

Yumyeongsan is fundamentally a granite “soil mountain,” but with rocky ridge and peak sections exposed along its summit area. According to the local peak registry, besides Yumyeongsan’s own summit, Eobisan (listed at two points, 849m/846m), Sogunisan (also two points, 798m/812m), and Jungmisan (849m) are all connected by ridgeline — so instead of just climbing Yumyeongsan itself, you can traverse the whole chain of peaks strung together along the ridge. The valleys here run wide and carry a good amount of water, and Yumyeongsan Valley in particular is a popular summer spot for wading and playing in the water. Down at the mountain’s base sits Yumyeongsan Recreational Forest, where the campground and forest roads sometimes overlap with the hiking trails. The summit itself is more of an open, ridge-shaped peak with sweeping views rather than a sharp, jagged pinnacle — though scattered rocky sections here and there still keep the hike from feeling monotonous.

What the Hike Feels Like

Photo: Korea Tourism Organization photo gallery (Uljin, Gyeongsangbuk-do) · Korea Open Government License

On most routes, the mood shifts in a fairly predictable arc as you go from the trailhead to the summit — forest, then valley, then ridge. The first stretch is usually a gentle path through a mix of broadleaf and conifer trees, shaded and a little humid. Following the valley upward brings a nice coolness, with the sound of water keeping you company the whole way. As you get closer to the ridge, the slope steepens and bare rock starts showing up, so the climb suddenly feels harder — and somewhere near the top there are usually spots where the trees open up just enough to catch a glimpse of the surrounding ridgelines. Spring brings fresh green leaves and royal azaleas, while in fall the foliage color plays off the valley water beautifully. Summer hikes here have that classic contrast: the valley section keeps you cool, but the humid forest path can get pretty sticky and warm.

Course 1 — Seoneochi Pass → Summit

📏 2.99km ⛰️ 383m gain ⏱️ 1:23 ➡️ One-way Difficulty ★★☆☆☆ ⤓ GPX

Route: Seoneochi Pass → fork in the trail → Soguni-san → toward Yumyeong-san → Yumyeong-san → valley course

Getting to the trailhead: Seoneochi Pass sits right on the border between Gapyeong and Yangpyeong counties, and since public transit doesn’t really serve this area, most people get here by car or taxi. Parking tends to mean pulling off onto the shoulder rather than using an actual lot, so it’s worth checking the width of the access road on a map app beforehand rather than counting on finding a proper parking space.

Highlights & who it’s for: This ridge route swings by Soguni-san first before climbing onto Yumyeong-san — short in distance and relatively gentle in elevation change, making it the most approachable of the six Yeodeok-area courses. You walk the ridge, then drop down via the valley course on the way back, so the ascent and descent feel like two completely different hikes. It’s a good pick if you’re new to hiking and want a taste of the summit ridge without committing a whole day, or if you’re just short on time.

Course 2 — Bokdong Intersection → Summit

📏 5.72km ⛰️ 781m gain ⏱️ 2:44 ➡️ One-way Difficulty ★★★★★ ⤓ GPX

Via: Bokdong Intersection → Pagoda Camp → Fork → Daebusan → Forest Road → Paragliding Site → High-Voltage Line → Gliding 2 → Yumyeongsan

Trailhead access: Bokdong Intersection is a bus stop listed in public data, so you can actually get to the trailhead by public transit. That said, there’s no dedicated parking data for the area, so if you’re driving, you’ll need to make do with whatever roadside space you can find — and on busy weekends, I’d honestly just take the bus.

Highlights & who it’s for: This course climbs past Daebusan, works its way along a forest road and through the paragliding launch area before reaching the summit. The elevation gain is substantial, and you’re on a steady uphill pretty much from the start, so it’s a course that’ll test your stamina. The payoff comes at the paragliding site, where the view opens up nicely. Overall, this is one of the go-to routes for hikers who’ve got the legs and the experience for it.

Course 3 — Second Ticket Booth Entrance → Summit

📏 11.25km ⛰️ 778m gain ⏱️ 4:07 ➡️ One-way Difficulty ★★★★★ ⤓ GPX

Route: Hanwha Condo Junction → Dongmakgol → Paragliding Park → Paragliding Site → Fork → Yangpyeong Cheongsan → Yumyeongsan Forest Road → Logging Area → Trail → Paragliding 1 → Paragliding 2 → Four Brothers Pine Trees → Marriage Tree → Second Ticket Booth Entrance → Ticket Booth 2

Getting to the trailhead: Hanwha Condo Junction has a registered bus stop, so it’s reachable by public transit. It’s an official trailhead with a ticket booth, but public data doesn’t show a dedicated parking lot, so if you’re driving, it’s a good idea to check ahead of time whether nearby shops or lodgings have room for parking.

Highlights and who it’s for: This is the longest course, passing through Dongmakgol and Paragliding Park before reaching named landmarks like the Four Brothers Pine Trees and the Marriage Tree. Even one-way, it takes nearly half a day. It’s best suited to hikers who want a well-rounded route covering a variety of terrain, or who are up for a long, physically demanding trek.

Course 4 — Fork 1 → Summit

📏 4.3km ⛰️ 538m gain ⏱️ 1:58 ➡️ One-way Difficulty ★★★☆☆ ⤓ GPX

Route: Fork 1 → Fork 2 → Fork 3 → Fork 4 → Recreation Forest Campground → Trailhead End Restroom → Parking Lot 1 → Ticket Booth 2 → Myeongsan Rest Garden → Parking Lot 2

Access to the trailhead: “Fork 1” isn’t a named place in its own right — it’s a junction on the trail that you reach by entering from the Yumyeongsan Natural Recreation Forest side. Since Parking Lots 1 and 2 are included along the route, most hikers actually use the recreation forest’s parking area as their real starting point.

Highlights and who it suits: This stretch links several forks with the recreation forest campground and parking lots, making it a good fit for a descent route or as part of a loop hike. It also passes near amenities like Myeongsan Rest Garden, so it works well for families who want somewhere to freshen up before or after the hike.

Course 5 — Trailhead/Valley Entrance → Yumyeongsan Summit

📏 3.21km ⛰️ 562m gain ⏱️ 1:44 ➡️ One-way Difficulty ★★★☆☆ ⤓ GPX

Route: Yumyeongsan 2nd Parking Lot → Solgarden, Sanjanggarden, Hwangtomadang restaurant row → restrooms → trailhead/valley entrance → 1st Parking Lot → Yumyeongsan Natural Recreation Forest Campground → Junction 7 → Junction 8 → Junction 9 → Junction 10 → Yumyeongsan Summit

Trailhead access: This route takes you past the Yumyeongsan 2nd Parking Lot and the nearby cluster of restaurants (Solgarden, Sanjanggarden, Hwangtomadang, and others) before entering at the mouth of the valley. With so many amenities concentrated right at the start, it’s an easy trailhead for grabbing a meal or getting your gear sorted before or after the hike. Access by car is also fairly convenient.

Highlights & who it’s for: True to its name, this course follows the valley up the mountain, with long stretches where you’re hiking to the sound of running water — making it especially popular in summer. Since it’s a short route that starts from a trailhead well stocked with amenities, it’s a great pick for hikers who want to combine valley trekking with a summit push.

Course 6 — Yumyeongsan Terminus → Summit

📏 9.91km ⛰️ 567m gain ⏱️ 3:25 ➡️ One-way Difficulty ★★★★★ ⤓ GPX

Route: Yumyeongsan Terminus → Deulkkotyeoul (Wildflower Shallows) → Eobisan Info Board → Fork → Ridge Trail → Hiking Trail → Viewpoint Rest Area → Near Eobisan → Eobisan → Eobi Valley Rest Area → Alice

Trailhead access: Yumyeongsan Terminus is registered in the public data as a bus terminus stop, which makes this one of the more accessible courses in the Yeodeok group when it comes to public transit. Since it’s an end-of-line stop, though, it’s worth checking the bus schedule ahead of time before you head out.

Highlights & who it’s for: This is a ridge-traverse course that starts at Deulkkotyeoul, climbs over Eobisan, and continues on to Yumyeongsan. Along the way you’ll pass a lookout point called the Viewpoint Rest Area, giving you the chance to take in both Eobisan and Yumyeongsan on a single hike. Both the distance and elevation gain are substantial, so you’ll need decent stamina — but since you can get to and from the trailheads entirely by public transportation, it’s the most practical pick for anyone planning a full ridge traverse.

Difficulty, Terrain, and Cautions

The trails within this area are registered under three sections based on administrative district (dong), and surface-condition data isn’t separately available.

According to the registered data, the highest difficulty rating across all sections comes out as “Easy” — but going by actual GPX tracks, there’s real variance on this mountain, with tougher stretches like the Bokdong-samgeori, Je-2 Maepyoso entrance, and Yumyeongsan-jongjeom courses climbing over 700m in net elevation gain. On the facilities side, the public data shows zero viewpoints, zero water stations, and zero shelters, so you should treat this as a mountain with no officially registered lookout points, drinking water sources, or emergency shelters. Hazard zones are also listed as zero, but that just means no separate hazardous section has been designated — it doesn’t mean the rocky ridges and valley stretches are actually risk-free, so you’ll still want to watch for rising water in the valley sections after rain and icing on the ridgeline in winter. There are 9 trailhead/exit points registered in total, so you’ve got a fairly wide range of route options to choose from.

Distance and Time

Photo: Korea Tourism Organization photo gallery (156-20 Jangsu-ro, Gapyeong-gun, Gyeonggi-do) · KOGL Type 1

The total trail length within the area comes to 19.51 km, adding up multiple routes — it’s not the distance of a single course but the combined length of all trails in the area, including the Gail-ri and Yongcheon-ri sections. Likewise, the data’s ascent/descent times of 373 minutes up and 260 minutes down are cumulative totals across the whole area, not the time for any one course, so don’t mistake them for a single-trail estimate. Working out the average pace from these numbers gives roughly 19 minutes per km climbing — worth cross-checking against GPX-based time estimates for individual courses (about 1 hour 23 minutes for the Seoneochi Pass route, and around 2 hours 44 minutes for the Bokdong-samgeori route) to help pick a course that matches your fitness level. Yumyeongsan is a mountain you can easily finish in a day trip — shorter routes take about half a day, while longer ridge-line courses like the one from the Second Ticket Booth entrance to the Yumyeongsan terminus can take a full day, so it’s best to start early and give yourself plenty of buffer. It’s not a mountain that calls for an overnight stay, but if you’re linking several courses into a through-hike, be sure to budget generously for the total time.

Getting There (Public Transit & Parking)

Photo: Korea Tourism Organization photo gallery (Bangil-ri, Seolak-myeon, Gapyeong-gun, Gyeonggi-do) · KOGL Type 1

Yumyeongsan isn’t directly served by the Seoul metropolitan subway, so the usual approach is to take the Gyeongchun Line to Cheongpyeong or Gapyeong Station and transfer to a local bus from there. According to public transit data (TAGO), the bus stops near the trailheads are Hanwha Condo Samgeori, Bokdong Samgeori, Sinbok 2-ri Bokdong Village, and Yumyeongsan Jongjeom (terminus) — and of these, Yumyeongsan Jongjeom and Bokdong Samgeori connect directly to the start of the trail, so you can begin your hike using public transit alone. That said, buses run pretty infrequently on this route, so it’s worth checking the schedule ahead of time. As for parking, public data shows zero officially registered lots, so if you’re driving, you’ll need to rely on the parking area at Yumyeongsan Recreational Forest or whatever roadside space you can find — and it’s worth keeping in mind that it can get crowded. For the most reliable, up-to-date directions and walking routes to the trailhead, KakaoMap or Naver Map is your best bet.

Season and mud, weather, and gear

Photo: Korea Tourism Organization Tourist Photo Gallery (63-18 Jangsu-ro 79beon-gil, Seorak-myeon, Gapyeong-gun, Gyeonggi-do) · KOGL Type 1

Spring and fall are your safest bets, weather- and visibility-wise — stable temperatures, clear views, and you get to enjoy fresh greenery or fall foliage alongside the valley streams. Summer is when the valley routes really come into their own, but it’s also humid, and a heavy rain can send the stream levels up fast, so if you’re planning to combine a dip in the water with your hike, keep a close eye on the forecast. Winter brings a real risk of ice on the ridgelines and rocky sections — and worth remembering, this isn’t a mountain with shelters or marked danger zones, so crampons, gaiters, and other winter gear aren’t optional, they’re essential. Across all seasons, you’ll want hiking boots, extra water (there are no drinking fountains on the mountain, so bring everything you’ll need), a waterproof jacket, trekking poles, and a headlamp if you’re tackling one of the longer routes.

Foreign Traveler Tips

Photo: Korea Tourism Organization Photo Gallery (654-58 Yumyeong-ro, Seorak-myeon, Gapyeong-gun, Gyeonggi-do) · KOGL

Yumyeongsan is romanized as “Yumyeongsan,” and it’s worth memorizing the names of the main trailheads beforehand—Bokdong-samgeori, Yumyeongsan Jongjeom (the terminus stop), and Hanwha Condo-samgeori—since they’ll come in handy when you’re figuring out buses. There’s generally no separate hiking permit or entrance fee, but if your route takes you through the Yumyeongsan Natural Recreation Forest, that area charges its own admission and facility-use fees, so it’s worth checking on-site or on the official website beforehand. Restrooms show up at a few points along the route (near the trail exit and around the valley entrance, for instance), but they’re not available the whole way, so it’s smart to use the facilities at the trailhead before you set off. There are no registered drinking-water points in the public data for this trail, so bring more water than you think you’ll need. As for maps, Google Maps tends to fall short on Korean hiking trail details, so I’d recommend installing KakaoMap or Naver Map—both widely used in Korea—to check the actual trail route and public transit connections. On the safety side, cell reception can get spotty along the ridge sections, so hiking with a companion is a good idea, and in an emergency you can reach Korea’s unified emergency number, 119.

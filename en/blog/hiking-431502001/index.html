Stone Pillars Arrayed Like a Folding Screen: Gwangju's Mudeungsan

Overview

Photo: Korea Tourism Organization photo gallery (Hwasun County, Jeonnam-Gwangju Integrated Special City) · KOGL

Mudeungsan spans the Dong-gu and Buk-gu districts of Gwangju along with Hwasun County in South Jeolla Province, and it’s the national park that best represents the Honam region. The summit itself is off-limits because of a military communications facility, so ordinary hikers can only climb as far as the columnar-joint formations near Seoseokdae and Ipseokdae. The total length of the trails within the park (a sum of several sections, not the distance of any single course) is 13.55 km, and according to Korea Forest Service data, the hardest of the registered sections is rated “easy.” That said, if you go by the measured GPX tracks for the country’s 100 famous mountains, the main routes up to the summit area run 5–9 km one way with a net elevation gain of 450–780 m, so they take a fair amount out of you — though they’re still easily doable as a day hike.

Terrain and Topography

Photo: Korea Tourism Organization photo gallery (Hwasun-gun, Jeonnam-Gwangju Integrated Special City) · KOGL

Mudeungsan has a distinctive shape: sizable rock peaks and columnar joint formations sit atop the gentle, rounded ridgeline of an earthen mountain. Up near the summit, Seoseokdae and Ipseokdae are columnar joints where stone pillars stand in a row like a folding screen — they’re well known for their scientific and scenic value, enough to have earned designation as a National Geopark. The major peaks registered in the public data include Manyeonbong (628m), Nuebong (1084m), Naktabong (938m), Anyangsan (876m), Manyeonsan (687m), Wonhyobong (583m), and Jungmeorijae (617m, recorded as 613m at some points). Based on the actual GPX measurements, the highest point these courses reach comes out to 1120m. The overall highest peak of the mountain is generally said to be Cheonwangbong (about 1187m), but access is restricted there because of communication facilities, so hikers often treat the area around Seoseokdae as their real “made it to the top” spot. Jigong-neodeol (a stretch where rocks form a boulder field) and the rocky terrain around Gyubongam are other features that give this mountain its character.

The Feel of the Climb

Photo: Korea Tourism Organization Photo Gallery (Hwasun-gun, Jeollagwangju Integrated Special City) · KOGL

The early part of the hike is pretty forgiving — dirt paths winding through dense cypress and broadleaf forest, with gentle stretches of ridgeline. But the higher you go, the tougher it feels, as scree fields and rocky ridges start creeping in. Around Jungbong and Jangbuljae, the silver grass meadows and open ridge views really bring out the mood of the season, and near Seoseokdae and Ipseokdae you get these wide, breathtaking scenes with columnar-jointed rock peaks rising right in front of you. In autumn it’s the silver grass and fall foliage that steal the show, while in winter the frozen ridgelines and hoarfrost tend to be the real spectacle.

Course 1 — Wonhyosa Entrance Bus Stop → Wonhyo Station Mountain Rescue Team

📏 6.75km ⛰️ 734m gain ⏱️ 2:55 ➡️ One-way Difficulty ★★★★★

Route: Wonhyosa parking lot → Mudeungsan rest area → Wonhyosa Entrance bus stop → Wonhyosa parking lot restroom → Songdo Sanjang → Mudeungsan Gaja → fork in the trail → Wonhyosa fork restroom → Wonhyo Station information center → Wonhyo Station Mountain Rescue Team → Wonhyo Station drinking-water spot → Wonhyo District restaurant strip → cypress forest → Kkomak-jae → steep section

Getting to the trailhead: City buses run toward Wonhyosa, so you can get off at the Wonhyosa Entrance stop and walk in from there. There’s parking nearby, but it can get crowded in peak season, so public transit is the way to go.

What it’s like / who it’s for: You start out on a gentle dirt path that winds through a cypress forest and the Wonhyo District restaurant strip, then the back half climbs over Kkomak-jae into a stretch of steep grade. It’s a good fit if you want a cypress-forest stroll and a proper hike rolled into one.

Course 2 — Dowon Village Parking Lot Entrance → Ipseokdae

📏 7.73km ⛰️ 775m gain ⏱️ 3:13 ➡️ One-way Difficulty ★★★★★

Route: Dowon Village visitor-only parking lot → Dowon Village parking lot entrance → Dowon Village information board → fork in the trail → Dowon Trail Support Center → restroom → Dowon Village–Gyubongam section trail marker → rest area → national point-location number → Gyubongam → Gyubongam prayer site → rock at the Gyubongam entrance → Jigong Neodeol → Seokburam → trail marker in front of Seokburam

Getting to the trailhead: For all practical purposes, the only way in is to use the visitor-only parking lot at Dowon Village, so most people drive here rather than take public transit.

Highlights & who it’s for: This course takes you past rocky terrain like Gyubongam and Jigong Neodeol, so you get to see both a temple and a boulder field in one go. Both the distance and the elevation gain are considerable, so it’s fairly demanding on your legs — a good fit for anyone who wants the quiet atmosphere of a mountain temple and a proper rocky-ridge hike at the same time.

Course 3 — Fork → Summit

📏 6.79km ⛰️ 610m gain ⏱️ 2:43 ➡️ One-way Difficulty ★★★★☆

Route: Fork → Dowon Village-Jangbuljae information board → rest area → national point number → Dowon Village-Jangbuljae section marker → viewpoint below Jungbong → Dowon Campground no-entry point → Neoreokbawi Yongso information board

Getting to the trailhead: This section starts from a mid-trail fork on another course, so there’s no clearly designated bus stop of its own. Most people reach it by linking up with the access road toward Dowon Village.

Highlights & who it’s for: A short, efficient stretch where you can take in the ridge views from the viewpoint below Jungbong. It suits hikers doing a through-hike that ties several courses together, or anyone short on time.

Course 4 — Wonhyosa Entrance → Summit

📏 9.39km ⛰️ 576m gain ⏱️ 3:18 🔁 Loop Difficulty ★★★★★

Passing through: Fork → National Point Number → Rest area → Old Trail Section 2 signpost → Jungbong Sarang-gil → Silver Grass Trail photo zone → Jungbong Silver Grass Trail viewpoint → Falling Rocks Warning → Icy Section → Mudeungsan Ice Rock → Signpost → Neutjae Rest Area → Wonhyosa Gate → Wonhyosa Entrance

Getting to the trailhead: You can get here on the same Wonhyosa-bound bus as Course 1.

Highlights & who it’s for: This route takes you past the silver grass photo zone and the Jungbong viewpoint, but it also includes a falling-rocks warning stretch and an icy section (the Mudeungsan Ice Rock), so you’ll want to be extra careful in winter. I’d recommend it for hikers who want to catch the autumn silver grass scenery.

Course 5 — Deulgukhwa Village Entrance, Suman-ri District 3 Stop → Seoseokdae

📏 8.21km ⛰️ 591m gain ⏱️ 3:02 ➡️ One-way Difficulty ★★★★☆

Route: Suman-ri Deulgukhwa Village visitor-only parking lot → Deulgukhwa Village Entrance, Suman-ri District 3 stop → Suman-ri Deulgukhwa Village → restroom at the Suman-ri Deulgukhwa Village parking lot → fork in the trail → trailhead information board → Deulgukhwa Village–Jangbuljae signpost → national point-location marker → rest area → ridge viewpoint → royal azalea colony → falling-rock caution → steep slope, rockfall danger → Baengma Ridge Trail → Neungseonam signpost

Getting to the trailhead: You can get off at the Suman-ri District 3 stop and walk right in, and there’s a visitor-only parking lot nearby you can use too.

Highlights & who it’s for: This is a ridge-style course that runs through a royal azalea colony and along the Baengma Ridge Trail, and it’s especially popular in spring when the azaleas are in bloom. There’s a steep section with a rockfall risk, so take extra care in wet weather.

Course 6 — Mudeungsan: Jeungsimsa → Jungbong

📏 7.72km ⛰️ 768m gain ⏱️ 3:13 🔁 Loop Difficulty ★★★★★

Route: Mudeungsan Jeungsimsa → fork → Obang Training Center → Dangsan Tree Songpungjeong rest area → Dangsan Tree → Dangsan Tree restrooms → wild boar warning → Jungmeorijae uphill rest area → lightning-prone area → Jungbong → Jungbong helipad → KBC transmitter → Donghwasa temple site rest area → Neutjae observation deck → washed-out trail section

Getting to the trailhead: The Jeungsimsa area is widely known as one of the easiest trailheads to reach by city bus from downtown Gwangju, and there’s a parking lot nearby too.

What it’s like / who it’s for: This is Gwangju’s signature course, and the one locals love most—it climbs past the forest around Jeungsimsa Temple and the old Dangsan tree up toward Jungmeorijae and Jungbong. There’s a marked lightning-prone stretch, so if a summer thunderstorm rolls in, it’s best to head back down. With its easy access and iconic status, it’s a great pick for first-time visitors.

Difficulty, Trail Surface & Cautions

According to Korea Forest Service data, the registered sections themselves are rated “Easy” — but that rating is based on the total length of the lowland stretches. The actual GPX-measured route all the way up to the summit ridge comes out at a top rating of ★★★★★, thanks to the sheer elevation gain and distance involved. The surface is largely clay, so you’ll want to be especially careful about how slippery it gets in the rain. Along the various courses you’ll also find real hazard signs: falling-rock warnings (courses 4 and 5), rockfall danger on steep slopes (course 5), icy stretches and Mudeungsan’s ice-covered rocks (course 4), and a lightning-prone area (course 6). Course 6 also has steep grades and washed-out trail sections, so after rain you really do need to check the surface conditions first. There’s one water point along the courses (the drinking-water spot at the Wonhyo station), and since no separate hazard-zone or viewpoint POIs are registered apart from the shelter, it’s best to rely on the signs and the information posted at the visitor centers.

거리와 소요시간

Photo: Korea Tourism Organization photo gallery (Hwasun-gun, Jeonnam-Gwangju Integrated Special City) · KOGL

Based on the trails within the area — a cumulative 239 minutes going up and 170 minutes coming down (these are totals across all sections, not the time for any single course) — the average ascent pace works out to about 18 minutes per kilometer. Most of the summit-area courses run 5–9 km one way with a net elevation gain of 450–780 m, so applying that pace puts the typical one-way time somewhere between roughly 2 hours and 3 hours 20 minutes. A round trip can take anywhere from 6 to 8 hours, so an early start is a good idea — but with no shelters and a total trail length of only about 13.55 km within the area, it’s perfectly doable as a day hike.

Getting There (Public Transit & Parking)

Photo: Korea Tourism Organization photo gallery (Hwasun County, Jeonnam-Gwangju Integrated Special City) · KOGL

Around downtown Gwangju, most people get around by city bus rather than subway. For the Wonhyosa side, you can use the “Mudeungsan National Park (Wonhyosa)” stop, while the Jeungsimsa side is typically reached by taking a bus toward the Jeungsimsa area. For the Sumanri side, get off at the Sumanri District 3 stop by the Deulgukhwa Village entrance. Since exact bus routes and schedules can vary depending on when you set out, it’s a good idea to check the real-time route on KakaoMap or Naver Map before you go. Public data lists zero registered parking lots (there are small local parking spots on-site, like the visitor lots at Dowon Village and Sumanri, but they’re limited), so on weekends and during peak season, taking public transit is the way to go.

Seasons, Weather, and Gear

Photo: Korea Tourism Organization photo gallery (Hwasun-gun, Jeonnam-Gwangju Integrated Special City) · KOGL

Spring brings royal azaleas, and autumn brings silver grass and fall foliage — those are the big draws, which is why spring and fall are considered the best seasons for hiking here. In summer, this area shows up as a lightning hotspot, so if there’s an afternoon thunderstorm in the forecast, it’s safest to skip the climb or head down early. In winter, there are genuine icy stretches and frozen-rock sections, so you’ll want crampons, gaiters, and other cold-weather gear. The ground is clay-heavy, so right after rain it’s a good idea to bring hiking boots and poles to keep from slipping. And no matter the season, pack plenty of water and snacks.

Tips for International Visitors

Photo: Korea Tourism Organization Photo Gallery (Hwasun-gun, Jeonnam-Gwangju Integrated Special City) · KOGL

Mudeungsan is written “Mudeungsan” in the Roman alphabet, and its main landmarks include Seosokdae, Ipseokdae, and Jungbong. It’s a national park area, and as far as I know there’s no separate entry permit or admission fee — though parts of the summit are off-limits because of communications facilities up there. There are restrooms at several spots: the Wonhyosa parking lot, the Wonhyosa trail junction, the Dowon Visitor Support Center, the Sumanri Wild Chrysanthemum Village parking lot, and near the Dangsan tree. For drinking water, there’s a water station at the Wonhyo branch office (a single fountain), but water facilities are pretty scarce overall, so it’s safest to bring plenty of your own. Google Maps often has inaccurate trail information within Korea, so I’d recommend using the KakaoMap or Naver Map apps while you’re here. You’ll actually see signs warning about falling rocks, icy sections, and lightning-prone areas, so be sure to follow whatever they say — and since there are no shelters, it’s important to plan your descent for before sunset.

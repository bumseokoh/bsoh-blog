Walking Busan's Godangbong Ridge Along the Geumjeongsanseong Fortress Walls

Hiking · Published 7/18/2026 ·

등산로 트랙·시설 (공공데이터: 전국등산로 공간정보) · 🚩시종점 💧음수대 👁️조망점 🏠대피소 🅿️주차장 ⚠️위험 · 지도 © OpenStreetMap ⤓ GPX 다운로드

Geumjeongsan (金井山) Hiking Guide

Geumjeongsan is the mountain that watches over Busan. It sits right on the edge of a bustling city, yet the moment you set foot on it, the noise falls away — and that’s exactly why so many locals love it. I’ve come here more than a few times myself, and honestly, it never gets old.

At the summit you’ll find Geumjeongsanseong Fortress, the largest mountain fortress in Korea. Walking along those old stone walls, you can’t help but feel the weight of history in your legs. Beomeosa Temple, tucked into the foothills, is another spot you really shouldn’t skip.

Getting There

The nice thing about Geumjeongsan is how easy it is to reach by public transit. Take the subway to Beomeosa Station, hop on a local bus, and you’re basically at the trailhead. No car needed — which, if you’ve ever circled a packed trailhead parking lot on a weekend, you’ll appreciate.

If you’d rather save your legs for the actual hiking, there’s a cable car too. It carries you a good chunk of the way up, so it’s a solid option for anyone traveling with kids or older family members.

Trails

There’s a route for just about everyone here, whatever your fitness level.

  • The Beomeosa course is the most popular. You climb up through the temple grounds, which makes for a scenic, unhurried walk.
  • The Sanseong course follows the fortress walls, so you get history and the views all at once.
  • If you’re pressed for time or just want an easy day, taking the cable car up and walking down is the way to go.

Whichever you pick, take your time and enjoy it. The mountain isn’t going anywhere.

Geumjeongsan is close enough to swing by on a whim, yet it gives you everything you want out of a real hike. If you find yourself in Busan, do yourself a favor and set aside a day for it.

개요

사진: 한국관광공사 관광사진 갤러리 (부산광역시 금정구 금성동) · 공공누리

Geumjeongsan straddles the Geumjeong, Buk, and Dongnae districts of Busan and the city of Yangsan in South Gyeongsang Province. It’s best known for Geumjeongsanseong Fortress, whose walls run along the ridgeline centered on the main peak, Godangbong. The total length of the registered trails within the district comes to 4.75 km, but that’s the combined figure for several courses rather than the distance of any single route — it’s an aggregate of the trail spatial data recorded for the administrative area. The data classifies the registered sections as easy, but don’t let that fool you: the summit-area courses I describe below cover real distance and serious elevation change, so they feel anything but easy on the legs. The trails are well maintained and public transit access is fairly convenient, making this a mountain you can comfortably tackle as a day hike.

산세와 지형

사진: 한국관광공사 관광사진 갤러리 (부산광역시 금정구 금성동) · 공공누리

Geumjeongsan is known as a mountain where granite bedrock breaks through the surface here and there, yet the overall character leans toward earthy trails and well-formed ridgelines — which is why it’s often described as a mountain that combines the qualities of both a “soil mountain” and a “rock mountain.” The summit area around Godangbong takes the form of a rocky peak, and along the ridgeline, one peak follows another — Janggunbong, Sanggyebong, Paribong, and more — as is commonly understood. Another defining feature is that the ridgeline itself overlaps with the walls of Geumjeongsanseong Fortress, so much of the hike runs right alongside the ramparts. Based on surveyed data, the highest point at the summit is confirmed to be 833m.

What the Hike Feels Like

Photo: Korea Tourism Organization photo gallery (Geumseong-dong, Geumjeong-gu, Busan) · KOGL

From what I’ve read, much of the trail runs through forest, so there are plenty of shaded stretches even in summer. Once you reach the ridge, the descriptions mention spots where the view opens up over the Busan cityscape, the Nakdong River, and out toward Yangsan. Up near the summit at Godangbong, the terrain turns rocky, and there may be sections where you’ll need to use your hands—a different kind of tension than the earth-and-forest stretches below. It’s a mountain that gets mentioned often for its fresh spring greenery along the fortress-wall path, and its silver grass and autumn foliage come fall.

Course 1 — Fork in the Trail → Godangbong Observatory

📏 9.88km ⛰️ 603m gain ⏱️ 3:29 ➡️ One-way Difficulty ★★★★★ ⤓ GPX

Route: Fork in the trail → Godang Spring → Godangbong Observatory → Godangbong Trailhead access: This section starts at a fork partway along the ridge, and it’s usually described as a continuation you reach after coming up from the North Gate of Geumjeongsanseong Fortress or the Sanseong Village side. There doesn’t seem to be a dedicated parking lot, so you’re probably better off parking along the roadside near Sanseong Village or just taking public transit. Highlights & who it’s for: This is the course where you’ll find a spot to top up your drinking water as you pass Godang Spring, and it suits hikers who want to enjoy both a ridge walk and the views from the observatory. It’s a long one, though, so you’ll want to pace yourself.

Course 2 — Beomeosa Temple parking lot → Godangbong Peak

📏 7.75km ⛰️ 628m gain ⏱️ 2:59 ➡️ One-way Difficulty ★★★★☆ ⤓ GPX

Route: Beomeosa parking lot → Geumjeong Fortress → Beomeosa Seon Culture Education Center → security office → Junction 11 → dangganjiju (flagpole supports) → parking lot (capacity 80) → Junction 14 → Junction 15 → restroom → Junction 18 → Junction 19 → Junction 20 → information board → Junction 22 Getting to the trailhead: The usual way to reach the temple entrance is to take Busan Metro Line 1 to Beomeosa Station, then transfer to a village bus or city bus. There’s also a large parking lot (room for about 80 cars) near the temple grounds, so driving your own car is fairly convenient too. Highlights & who it’s for: This is one of Geumjeongsan’s signature courses, climbing a wooded trail that winds past the thousand-year-old Beomeosa Temple and blends in with the mountain fortress. There’s a restroom partway up, which makes it relatively manageable even for first-timers and families. Recommended for anyone who wants to take in both the temple and the fortress ruins in one go.

Course 3 — Hopo New Village → Summit

📏 7.91km ⛰️ 738m gain ⏱️ 3:12 ➡️ One-way Difficulty ★★★★★ ⤓ GPX

Waypoints: Hopo New Village → Rescue Marker Geumjeongsan 1-1 → Yongdeok Farm → Rescue Marker Geumjeongsan 1-2 → Signpost → Development Restriction Zone Notice → Junction 7 → Rescue Markers Geumjeongsan 1-3 to 1-7 → Junctions 13, 15 → Rescue Marker Dongmyeon Forest Road 6-28 Trailhead access: This route is known for starting from the village near Hopo Station on Busan Metro Line 2. There’s no large parking facility to speak of, so public transit is the way to go. Character & who it’s for: With a net elevation gain of 738m, this is one of the steepest of the summit-area courses and takes a real toll on your legs. What sets it apart is how it gradually shifts from village lanes to forest roads and then mountain trails. Best suited to hikers with some experience under their belt.

Course 4 — Fork 5 → Observation Deck

📏 6.13km ⛰️ 715m gain ⏱️ 2:43 ➡️ One-way Difficulty ★★★★☆ ⤓ GPX

Via: Fork 5 → Hopo Sae-dongne → Yangsan Nuri-gil → Geumhosa Temple → Yisu Grave → Pavilion → Bench·Exercise Equipment → Fork 12 → Hopo Farm → Rescue Marker Geumjeong 2-1 → Vehicle-Control Pipe → Signpost·Information Board → Bench·Rescue Marker → Fork 18 → Bench·Signpost Getting to the trailhead: Like Course 3, you approach from the Hopo side, and since it runs through a stretch that connects to the Yangsan Nuri-gil, it’s worth checking the Yangsan-side access road too. Highlights & who it’s for: With a pavilion, benches, exercise equipment, and several other spots to catch your breath, this one is described as a gentle, easy-going course with plenty of chances to rest along the way. It’s a good fit for hikers who want to tie it in with a walk along the Yangsan Nuri-gil.

Course 5 — Cheongnyeonam Side Path → Summit

📏 10.14km ⛰️ 746m gain ⏱️ 3:47 ➡️ One-way Difficulty ★★★★★ ⤓ GPX

Waypoints: Cheongnyeonam side path → pagoda → electric fence → no-entry section → fork 5 → Naewonam signboard → forks 7·8·9 → no-entry sign → cypress grove → steel fence → forks 14·15 → wetland protection area Trailhead access: This course starts near Cheongnyeonam temple, and it’s generally understood to connect with the access road on the Hwamyeong-dong side of Buk-gu. If you’re taking public transit, it’s safest to check the local city bus routes before you head out. Highlights & who it’s for: The big draw here is passing through a cypress grove and a wetland protection area. Some sections are marked off-limits, so be sure to respect the signs. It’s a fascinating course for hikers with an interest in natural ecosystems.

Course 6 — Dabang Three-Way Junction → Dabangbong

📏 10.54km ⛰️ 726m gain ⏱️ 3:51 ➡️ One-way Difficulty ★★★★★ ⤓ GPX

Route: Dabang Three-Way Junction → Dabangcheon Information Board → Gyeseok Village Bus Stop → Marker Stone (Gyeseok Village) → Fork 5 → Geukdong Restaurant → Fork 7 → Right-hand Path by Daejeong Apartments → Fork 9 → Signpost → Pylon → Rest Platform → Fork 13 → Wild Boar Caution Section → Wildfire Zone Getting to the trailhead: This course starts out from the Gyeseok Village area in Dabang-dong, Yangsan. Since a village bus stop is one of the waypoints, it looks like you can get here by public transit reasonably well. Highlights & who it’s for: The layout is a little unusual, running from village lanes all the way up to a wildfire zone. There’s a warning about wildlife (wild boars) in the area, so I’d suggest hiking with company rather than going it alone.

Course 7 — Geumsan Humansia Bus Stop → Summit

Getting to the trailhead: This route starts at a bus stop near an apartment complex. At 12.61km, it’s the longest of all the summit routes — pretty much an all-day hike. What it’s like & who it’s for: As the longest route in both distance and time, it suits hikers who are confident in their fitness. For a round trip you won’t need to camp overnight, but I’d recommend heading out early in the morning.

Course 8 — Jijang-am Entrance → Summit

Getting to the trailhead: This route starts near the temple Jijang-am. With a net elevation gain of 475m, it’s on the gentler side among the summit-bound routes. What it’s like / who it’s for: Since both the distance and the elevation gain are relatively small, this is one of the summit routes that beginners can take on with comparatively less strain.

Just to be clear, the table above is the combined total length of all the trails in the district, not the distance of any single course. The surface is mostly packed dirt, but the area around the summit of Godangbong is said to be granite ridge terrain, so you’ll want to be extra careful about slipping when it rains or when things ice over in winter. According to the facilities data, there are zero danger zones, zero viewpoints, zero trailhead/exit signposts, and zero water fountains on record — so it’s safest to check your location using both the on-site markers and a map app.

Distance and Time Required

Photo: Korea Tourism Organization photo gallery (Geumseong-dong, Geumjeong-gu, Busan) · KOGL

The summit routes run anywhere from about 7km on the short end to 12.6km on the long end, and most work out to roughly three or four hours one way — so a round trip is easily a day hike you can finish and be home the same day. If you add up every registered section within the district, the cumulative time comes to 82 minutes going up and 59 coming down. That figure isn’t the time for any one particular course, though — it’s the total across all the sections in the district combined. Working backwards from it, the average uphill pace comes out to about 17 minutes per km. When you’re pacing yourself on an actual summit route, that’s a handy number to keep in the back of your mind.

가는 길(대중교통·주차)

사진: 한국관광공사 관광사진 갤러리 (부산광역시 금정구 금성동) · 공공누리

From Beomeosa Station on Busan Metro Line 1, you can transfer to a bus or village bus headed toward Beomeosa and reach the trailhead for Course 2 (Beomeosa parking lot → Godangbong). And from near Hopo Station on Line 2, the routes are said to connect to Courses 3 and 4 (toward Hopo Saedongne). For the exact transfer routes and stop locations, it’s best to pull up a live route on KakaoMap or Naver Map before you set out. The public transit data lists a handful of stops near the trails — Singeomdan Jungang Station Intersection (Tunnel Entrance), Dietre The Hill (Gosan Tunnel), Paragon Botanic Park (Gosan Tunnel), Prugio The Venue Rear Gate, and Dietre The Hill·Ieum High School — which you can use as a reference, but do check the distance between each stop and the trailhead again on a map app before you actually rely on them. As for parking, aside from the lot near Beomeosa (room for about 80 cars), I couldn’t find any separate large facility, so I’d recommend using public transit if you can.

Season, Weather, and Gear

Photo: Korea Tourism Organization photo gallery (Geumseong-dong, Geumjeong-gu, Busan) · KOGL

Spring and fall are generally the easiest seasons for hiking. In summer, you’ll want to be ready for humidity, sweltering heat, and sudden showers that come out of nowhere. In winter, the rocky ridge sections near the summit can ice over, so chain crampons and cold-weather gear are a must. For the basics, bring hiking boots, a spare set of clothes, plenty of water (there are almost no drinking fountains along the way, so I’d recommend carrying at least 1–1.5L), a headlamp, and a rain poncho or waterproof jacket.

Tips for Foreign Travelers

Photo: Korea Tourism Organization photo gallery (Geumseong-dong, Geumjeong-gu, Busan) · KOGL

The summit is marked as Godangbong (고당봉), and the mountain itself as Geumjeongsan (금정산). As far as I know, you don’t need a special hiking permit or entrance fee, but temple grounds like Beomeosa may charge a separate cultural-heritage admission fee, so check the on-site signs. There’s a restroom about halfway along Course 2 (Beomeosa parking lot → Godangbong), but no drinking fountains are listed in the public data, so be sure to bring your own water. Google Maps is often unreliable for hiking trails in Korea, so it’s safer to install KakaoMap or Naver Map ahead of time to check the actual route and public transit. Watch your footing on the rocky ridge near the summit, and plan to set out with plenty of time to spare so you’re not still coming down after dark.

Photo: Korea Tourism Organization Photo Gallery (Incheon Metropolitan City) · Gongnuri

The Ara Bike Path traces the Gyeongin Ara Waterway, the channel linking Incheon’s West Sea coast to Seoul’s Hangang River, making for a flat, riverside ride from start to finish. What I love about it is how one-directional it feels — you start with sea breeze in your face and end up following the Han, so there’s a real sense of “here’s where I began, here’s where I finished.” Most of the route runs along a dedicated bike path right beside the water, so there are almost no hills, and the pavement stays smooth and even the whole way — easy enough for beginners or anyone getting back in the saddle after a long break.

There are really two ways to make use of this route.

  • A day-trip out-and-back: since it’s 21km one way, you can start from either Jeongseojin or the Han River side, ride to the far end, and turn around — a solid half-day outing. Even with a few rest stops along the way, you can comfortably finish in an afternoon.
  • First leg of the cross-country route, or a longer tour: the Ara Bike Path is part of the official cross-country certification route (Guknto Jongju). Link it up at the Ara Han River Lock with the Hangang and Namhangang bike paths, and you’re on the opening stretch of the ride that eventually reaches all the way to Busan — a good place to stamp your very first certification of the tour.

You can also work it into other setups — bring a folding bike like a Brompton or a mini-velo, take public transit to one end, and ride the route one-way only, or just do a short flat stretch with family or beginners in the group.

Course Info

Photo: Korea Tourism Organization photo gallery (Incheon) · Gongu Nuri

The route runs between two endpoints: Jeongseojin (Ara Seohae Gapmun), where it meets the West Sea at Incheon, and Ara Hangang Gapmun, where it meets the Han River in Seoul. Between the two locks, the Gyeongin Ara Waterway — an artificial canal — cuts a straight line, with a dedicated bike path running along the banks on either side, so you ride right alongside the water the whole way.

Starting from the Jeongseojin side, you begin against the backdrop of the West Sea, the lock, and wide-open sky, then gradually head inland past the Ara Tower observatory and along the canal. Go the other direction, starting from Ara Hangang Gapmun, and you open with the refreshing view of where the Han River meets the waterway before heading out toward the sea. Since almost the entire route sticks to flat, dedicated bike paths beside the canal, you rarely have to mix with car traffic, and the bridges crossing the waterway along with the lock structures work as natural landmarks, so it’s hard to lose your way.

Being a certified route, there are certification centers built right into the flow of the ride. The Jeongseojin (Ara Seohae Gapmun) certification center anchors the sea end and the Ara Hangang Gapmun certification center anchors the river end, with three more centers spaced along the way so you can stamp your book as you go. Both end-point locks are landmarks in their own right, each with a spacious plaza and sculptures, making them great spots to knock out your start/finish stamps and grab a few photos to remember the ride by.

Nearby Places to Eat

Photo: Korea Tourism Organization photo gallery (Incheon) · KOGL Type 1

Since the Ara Waterway trail runs through Incheon’s Seo-gu and Gyeyang districts on one end and Gimpo/Seoul’s Gangseo area on the other, your food options really open up once you roll into the city centers at either tip rather than along the course itself. On the Incheon side, you’ll typically find the local specialties — jjolmyeon (chewy cold noodles), “washbasin” naengmyeon served in giant bowls, muteombeong (spicy monkfish stew) — plus jjajangmyeon and its relatives, which trace back to the city’s Chinese immigrant community. Head toward Gimpo or Gangseo, and the urban stretches there are generally lined with everyday staples like baekban (home-style rice sets), gukbap (soup with rice), and bunsik (Korean street-food snacks). Rather than chasing one specific restaurant, I’d just plan to finish the ride, wander a few minutes into whichever downtown you land near, and dig into whatever that area’s known for.

One thing worth flagging: the stretch along the waterway itself is basically a supply desert. Once you get past the locks and observation decks, convenience stores and restaurants thin out fast, so it’s smart to stock up on water and snacks at either city center or near the locks before heading out. On hot days especially, load up on supplies before you start — you don’t want to be caught short out there.

Supplies & Amenities

Here’s the facility rundown: 3 certification centers, 16 restrooms, 1 water station, and 0 air pumps. Sixteen restrooms is a generous number for a riverside route, so it’s easy to time your breaks and quick fixes. That said, there’s only one water station and no air pumps at all, so bring plenty of your own drinking water, and don’t skip a portable pump and puncture repair kit in case of flats or low tire pressure.

Since this is a certified route, you’ll want to use the certification passport (cycling journey booklet). Stamp it at the three certification centers along the way to log your completion — at staffed centers, get your stamp at the counter, while at unstaffed ones, you stamp it yourself at the stamp booth provided. The centers at Jeongseojin (Ara West Sea Lock) and Ara Hangang Lock, at either end of the route, serve as your start and finish stamps.

It’s mostly a dedicated bike path, but around the lock gates, the observation deck, and the parks there are shared sections where pedestrians and cyclists mix, so slow down and give walkers the right of way. Since the route runs along an open waterway with little shade, summer brings a real risk of dehydration and heatstroke from the heat radiating off the pavement and direct sun. There’s only one water station on the whole route, so drink often and plan your refill stops ahead of time. In winter and early spring, ice can linger on bridges and shaded sections, so slow down there too. Visibility gets poor around the lock structures and at early morning or dusk, so always run your headlight and taillight — and if you’re riding at night, a reflective vest is a good idea.

Season, Weather & Gear

Photo: Korea Tourism Organization photo gallery (Incheon) · Korea Open Government License

The best seasons for this route are spring and fall, when wind and temperatures are mild. The flat course really shines during these months, making it comfortable even for beginners. In summer, the exposed riverside stretch can get brutal with heat and strong sun, so ride early morning or evening and bring plenty of water. Winter brings biting riverside winds and the chance of icy patches, so dress warm and watch your footing—er, wheels. River and canal routes can also kick up strong headwinds at any time of day, so it’s worth picking your outbound direction based on wind and how much gas you’ve got left in the tank.

Must-have gear: a helmet, front and rear lights, a puncture kit with a portable pump, and plenty of water. Since there’s no air pump station along this route, that portable pump really earns its keep. Add sunscreen, a light windbreaker, gloves, and some quick snacks, and you’re set for any season.

Tips for Foreign Travelers

사진: 한국관광공사 관광사진 갤러리 (인천광역시) · 공공누리

① Route name: This route is the Ara Bike Path (아라자전거길), running between Jeongseojin / Ara Seohae Gapmun (정서진·아라서해갑문, on the West Sea side) and Ara Hangang Gapmun (아라한강갑문, on the Han River side). The waterway itself is labeled the Gyeongin Ara Waterway (경인아라뱃길).

② A practical guide to the certification system (this route qualifies): Korea’s bike path certification system lets you log your rides with stamps as you complete each section.

  • You buy your Bike Passport in person at a staffed certification center. The official certification website is essentially Korean-only, and between foreign credit card issues and Korea’s identity-verification requirements, buying one in advance from abroad is basically not going to happen. Your best bet is just to pick one up at a staffed center once you’re actually in Korea.
  • Unmanned certification centers look like little red British phone booths, and each one has a stamp and ink pad waiting inside. Just stamp the corresponding box in your passport yourself. Most of these booths are accessible 24 hours a day, so you can get your stamp whenever, regardless of staffed-office hours.
  • Finish a section and you get a certification sticker to add to your passport. Complete an entire route — like the full cross-country circuit — and you’ll typically be awarded a completion certificate and medal. The Ara Bike Path happens to be the starting leg of that circuit.

③ If you’re arriving without a bike: no bike of your own, no problem — just rent locally. In the Seoul area, the public bike-share system Ttareungi is everywhere, and since it has an English-language app and takes foreign cards, it’s fairly easy for visitors to use. That said, public bikes are really built for getting around the city rather than long-distance touring, so if you’re planning to actually ride the full course, you’re better off renting a road or hybrid bike from a private rental shop.

④ Map apps: Google Maps often gets bike directions wrong in Korea. Switch to cycling mode in KakaoMap or Naver Map instead — both give much more accurate guidance on dedicated bike paths and certification center locations. Both apps support an English interface, and searching with the romanized place names given above should make it easy to find what you’re looking for.

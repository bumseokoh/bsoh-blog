Silver Grass Plains and Rocky Ridge Views: Sinbulsan in Ulsan

개요

Photo: Korea Tourism Organization photo gallery (Sangbuk-myeon, Ulju-gun, Ulsan) · KOGL

Sinbulsan straddles the boundary between Ulju-gun in Ulsan and Yangsan in South Gyeongsang Province, and it’s one of the signature peaks that make up the so-called “Yeongnam Alps” ridge, alongside Gajisan, Ganwolsan, and Yeongchuksan. The summit sits at around 1,159m according to the trail signs, though actual GPS readings come in somewhere between 1,170 and 1,220m depending on the route — a discrepancy that likely comes down to differences between where the summit marker stands and where nearby peaks (Ganwolsan summit at 1,086m, Baenaebong summit at 993m, and so on) were measured. The mountain as a whole has a mix of gentle ridgelines, silver grass plains, and rocky outcrops scattered here and there, which puts it somewhere between an earthy mountain and a rocky one. Difficulty is “moderate” overall, but some routes include long stretches that run up to six hours one way, so you’ll want to pace yourself. Most summit routes can be done as a day hike, but if you want to traverse the entire trail network in the area (73.11km in total), you should plan on at least one overnight stay.

산세와 지형

Photo: Korea Tourism Organization photo gallery (Sangbuk-myeon, Ulju-gun, Ulsan) · Korea Open Government License

Sinbulsan is a mountain that combines the features of a gently ridged “earth mountain” with exposed rocky ridges near the summit. The ridge running west from the peak is widely known as the “Sinbulsan silver grass plateau,” a broad expanse of grassland, and in autumn this stand of silver grass is one of the mountain’s signature sights. Bare rock crops up here and there around the summit, opening up the views, while the valleys and lower slopes are thickly forested — the classic look of an earth mountain. The main peaks registered in the geospatial data include Ganwolsan (1,086m), Baenaebong (993m), and Sinbulsan itself (several points in the roughly 1,170–1,220m range), and these peaks link up along the ridgeline to form the backbone of the Sinbulsan–Ganwolsan traverse.

The trails within the district are registered as six sections by administrative dong. These are divided by the administrative unit the trail passes through rather than by trail name, so they shouldn’t be mistaken for individual course names. Below is the total length registered for each section.

The Feel of the Climb

Photo: Korea Tourism Organization photo gallery (Sangbuk-myeon, Ulju-gun, Ulsan) · KOGL

What makes hiking Sinbulsan distinctive is the way it flows: you start out in dense, wooded trail at the base and gradually open onto ridgeline with wide views. The lower section runs mostly alongside a valley, so in summer you get shade and the sound of running water together, and as you near the summit, stretches of bare rock and silver-grass fields take turns, opening up views in every direction. Along the ridge near the top especially, the mountains of the Yeongnam Alps spread out like a panorama — the general take is that the payoff at the summit is big precisely because the climb takes so much out of you. It’s known as a mountain that wears its seasons plainly: fresh green and azaleas in spring, silver grass in fall. In winter, though, biting winds along the ridge and icy patches show up, which can be a lot to handle for beginners.

Course 1 — Restroom → Summit

📏 16.99km ⛰️ 1041m gain ⏱️ 5:59 🔁 Loop Difficulty ★★★★★ ⤓ GPX

Waypoints: Restroom → Tourist Site → Spring → Rest Area → Trail Map → Parking Lot → Natural Scenery → Junction 13 → Junction 17 → Information Center → Viewpoint → Junction 20 → Junction 21 → Junction 23 → Junction 24

Getting to the trailhead: Since there’s a restroom, parking lot, and information center clustered right at the start, this looks like a properly maintained trailhead with a developed access road. If you’re coming by public transit, the usual approach is to walk from the nearest bus stop and follow the signs in. It’s worth double-checking the exact stop name and how often the buses run with a map app before you head out.

What it’s like / who it’s for: This is the longest one-way route of the bunch (about 17km), so it’s a real long-haul course. The upside is that there are quite a few facilities spread along the way — a spring, rest areas, an information center — so even if it takes a while to finish, you won’t struggle to find a spot to catch your breath. It suits anyone who’s confident in their fitness and can set aside a whole day for the hike. If you’re planning a round trip in a single day, an early-morning start is the way to go.

코스 2 — 들머리 → 정상

📏 12.27km ⛰️ 992m gain ⏱️ 4:43 🔁 Loop Difficulty ★★★★★ ⤓ GPX

경유: 신불산터널(공사 막바지 구간) → 신불사 → 위험 → 갈림길 6 → 계곡 → 갈림길 9 → 자연경관 → 안전구조판 → 조망점 → 갈림길 27 → 복운사 → 들머리

들머리 접근: The trailheads pass temples like Sinbulsa and Bogunsa, which suggests this is the approach route from the Baenaegol side — and it’s known to be a fairly convenient stretch if you’re driving your own car. If you’re coming by public transit, the usual approach is to take a bus that runs through one of the nearby villages and then walk in the rest of the way.

특징·추천대상: This course mixes valleys, temples, and lookout points fairly evenly, so the scenery keeps changing in a way that really stands out. That said, since a “danger” marker actually shows up on the signposts, you’ll want to be careful in wet weather or during the freezing season. It’s a good fit for anyone who wants to soak in both the temple culture and the mood of the valley at the same time.

Course 3 — Entrance → Seokjeongbong Summit

📏 12.61km ⛰️ 857m gain ⏱️ 4:35 ➡️ One-way Difficulty ★★★★★ ⤓ GPX

Waypoints: Paid parking → scenic viewpoint → restrooms → tourist attraction → café → restaurant → bus stop → entrance → rest area → junction 10 → lookout point → Seokjeongbong summit

Getting to the trailhead: With the paid parking, café, restaurant, and bus stop all clustered together, this looks to be the access road near the Deungeok Alpsri hot spring resort area, and it’s one of the easier Sinbulsan courses to reach whether you’re coming by public transit or by car.

What it’s like and who it’s for: The trail starts by passing through a well-developed tourist area before heading up into the mountains, so it’s easy to get to even for families or travelers who just want a quick look around the lower stretch. Past the lookout point, though, it’s a steady climb all the way to the Seokjeongbong summit, so if you’re planning to go the whole way, you’ll want to pace yourself.

Course 4 — Trailhead → Gojangsan Summit

📏 5.54km ⛰️ 899m gain ⏱️ 2:53 ➡️ One-way Difficulty ★★★★★ ⤓ GPX

Waypoints: Parking lot → Café → Rest area → Scenic view → Hazard → Lookout point → Helipad → Junction 40 → Junction 41 → No-entry → Trail map → Gojangsan summit → Trailhead → Fishing spot

Getting to the trailhead: With a parking lot and café right next to it, this looks like an easy trailhead to reach by car. A fishing spot is marked nearby too, which makes me think it’s the access road into a village near a reservoir or a mountain stream.

What it’s like / who it’s for: At 5.5 km one way with 899 m of net elevation gain, this course packs a fairly steep grade for its distance. It runs past a helipad and a lookout point, so the views are good, but there’s a stretch marked “No-entry” — be sure to check on-site whether that section is actually closed off.

Course 5 — Rest Shelter → Summit

📏 2.87km ⛰️ 777m gain ⏱️ 2:01 ➡️ One-way Difficulty ★★★★☆ ⤓ GPX

Route: Rest Shelter → Viewpoint → Scenic Overlook → Junction 19

Getting to the trailhead: Given how sparse the signposting is, I’d guess this is a short connector that cuts straight up to the summit from a midpoint on an existing trail. I couldn’t find any public transit stops for it, so people generally tackle it in combination with another course.

What it’s like / who it’s for: At 2.87km one way with a net elevation gain of 777m, this is the shortest course of the bunch by distance, but it’s also one of the steepest. It’s a good fit if you want summit views without a big time commitment, though there are plenty of steep stretches — so watch your knees and your footing on the way down.

Course 6 — Safety Rescue Marker → Summit

📏 7.88km ⛰️ 901m gain ⏱️ 3:28 ➡️ One-way Difficulty ★★★★★ ⤓ GPX

Route: Safety rescue marker → Fork 21 → Danger → Safety rescue marker & first-aid box → Scenic view → Fork 25 → Fork 27 → Lookout point → Safety rescue marker → Rough trail → Scenic view

Getting to the trailhead: The trailhead is logged under the name of a safety fixture (a safety rescue marker) rather than an actual place name, so this looks like an access path with no official designation. Before you set out, it’s safest to check the real entry point against an on-site trail map or a mapping app.

What it’s like / who it’s for: With “Danger” and “Rough trail” cropping up repeatedly on the signposts, the footing tends to be rough throughout and there are plenty of spots that call for caution. This one suits hikers with some experience rather than casual walkers, and it’s best avoided right after rain or during the freezing season.

난이도·노면·주의

사진: 한국관광공사 관광사진 갤러리 (울산광역시 울주군 상북면) · 공공누리

According to the Korea Forest Service’s trail spatial data, the highest difficulty rating for the trails within Sinbulsan is “moderate,” and the surface material isn’t listed separately. That said, the field-measured GPX data for Korea’s 100 Famous Mountains shows that many of the summit-area routes involve a net elevation gain of over 800m and take anywhere from three to six hours one way — physically demanding stuff. So it’s worth keeping in mind that the actual difficulty you’ll feel on the ground may be higher than the “moderate” label on the official record suggests.

By the facility statistics, the counts for viewpoints, water fountains, shelters, and hazardous sections all come out to zero, so there are no officially registered convenience or safety facilities on record. That said, the GPX waypoint names show “viewpoint” and “hazard” markers cropping up repeatedly on routes 2, 3, 4, and 6 — which suggests that while these aren’t reflected in the formal facilities database, they may well exist on site in the form of signboards. On the summit ridge and exposed rock sections, you’ll want to be especially careful of slippery footing in the rain and of icing and strong winds in winter, and it’s best to avoid pushing on recklessly through any stretch marked “rough trail” or “hazard.”

거리와 소요시간

사진: 한국관광공사 관광사진 갤러리 (울산광역시 울주군 상북면) · 공공누리

The cumulative times logged for the district’s entire trail network come to 1,389 minutes going up and 962 minutes coming down. That’s not the time for any single course, though — it’s the total for every registered section in the district added together. Convert that to distance and you get an average uphill pace of roughly 19 minutes per kilometer, which tells you that Sinbulsan’s trails are mostly steady climbs rather than gentle strolls. For the actual time on each course, you’re better off checking the GPX-measured table I gave earlier. To pull out a couple of examples, the easy-to-reach Entrance → Seokjeongbong Summit course takes about 4 hours 35 minutes one way, while the shortest one, Rest Area → Summit, runs somewhere around 2 hours. Most courses can be done as a day hike even round-trip, but if you want to thru-hike the full 73.11 km that links every section in the district, it’s safer to plan on at least one overnight.

Getting There (Public Transit & Parking)

Photo: Korea Tourism Organization photo gallery (Sangbuk-myeon, Ulju-gun, Ulsan) · KOGL

There are several bus stops registered near Sinbulsan, including Tongdosa Main Gate, Sujeong Beopbok, Jangje Hall, Baengnyeon Village, Taebong Village, and Jangje Village. Of these, the Tongdosa Main Gate stop makes a handy reference point if you’re approaching the trailhead from the Yangsan side. From downtown Ulsan or the Yangsan area, the usual approach is to take an intercity or city bus to that stop and then walk to whichever trailhead you’re headed for. If you’re heading toward the Deungeok Alps-ri hot spring complex (Course 3), I’ve read that catching a bus bound for the hot spring complex is the more convenient option.

The official data lists zero registered parking lots, but that figure is based on the Korea Forest Service’s trail spatial data — in practice, there are known to be paid parking lots near the hot spring complex and the temple (the Course 3 signage does mark a “paid parking lot”). For accurate bus schedules and real-time directions, I’d recommend searching your destination on KakaoMap or Naver Map; both apps are far better at guiding you through local public transit and walking routes in Korea.

Season, Weather, and Gear

Photo: Korea Tourism Organization photo gallery (Sangbuk-myeon, Ulju-gun, Ulsan) · KOGL

The easiest seasons to hike here are spring (April–May) and fall (September–October). Spring brings fresh greenery and azaleas, while fall is all about the silver grass swaying along the summit ridge. In summer, you’ll want to be ready for the humidity and heat down in the valley sections, plus the odd sudden downpour — and since there’s no marked water fountain to rely on, bring plenty of drinking water. In winter, the ridgeline can get hit with strong winds and icy patches, so crampons, gaiters, and a windproof jacket are essential. Some sections are marked “rough trail” or “danger,” so if you’re a beginner, it’s best to avoid hiking alone in winter. For basic gear, I’d recommend hiking boots, a spare set of clothes, a headlamp, some emergency food, and a map app.

Tips for Foreign Travelers

Photo: Korea Tourism Organization photo gallery (Sangbuk-myeon, Ulju-gun, Ulsan Metropolitan City) · KOGL

Sinbulsan is written “Sinbulsan” in Roman letters, with the summit rendered as “Sinbulsan-jeongsang (Sinbulsan Peak).” Nearby peaks can be written alongside it too: Ganwolsan and Baenaebong. As far as I know, there’s no climbing permit required and no separate entrance fee, but if your route passes through a temple’s grounds (Sinbulsa, Bogunsa, and the like), it’s good manners to be respectful during your visit. You’ll find restrooms at some of the trailheads (Courses 1 and 3), but there are none up at the summit, so it’s worth going before you set off. There are no officially registered drinking-water stations either, so bringing your own water is a must. Google Maps is often unreliable for hiking trails in Korea, so I’d recommend using KakaoMap or Naver Map to check the actual route on the ground. Some ridge sections can have spotty cell reception during the hike, so it’s safer to save your route offline beforehand and let people around you know who you’re going with and when you expect to be back down.

Overview

Photo: Korea Tourism Organization Photo Gallery · Park Jeong-ah (Gwacheon, Gyeonggi Province) · Gonggonuri

Gwanaksan (657m) is one of the most iconic mountains in southern Seoul, spanning Gwanak-gu and Geumcheon-gu in the city, as well as Gwacheon and Anyang in Gyeonggi Province. The summit, Yeonjudae, is famous for the small hermitage (Eungjin-jeon) perched on a sheer cliffside, and as an “urban” rocky mountain, it is incredibly easy to access from almost anywhere in Seoul. Since many trails lead directly from subway stations to the trailhead, you can easily reach them using just public transit. Most routes take between 2.5 to 3.5 hours one way, making it a perfect choice for a day hike. While the total length of all trails in the area reaches 43.69km (the sum of various courses, not a single continuous path), there is plenty of variety to choose from. Although the difficulty is officially rated as “moderate,” don’t let that fool you—the rocky terrain can make it feel much more strenuous depending on which section you’re tackling.

Terrain and Topography

Gwanaksan is a classic golsan—a “bony mountain” where the rocky skeleton of the peaks is clearly exposed. Because it is covered in granite crags and ridges, it’s often nicknamed “Little Seorak.”

The main highlights include the summit’s Yeonjudae (657m), the eight peaks of the Palbong ridge, Hakbawi Gukgibong, and the flagpole peak on the Jaunam Ridge. One of the most iconic sights of Gwanaksan is seeing those various flagpoles with the Taegeukgi (Korean national flag) planted along the ridges.

You’ll encounter all sorts of uniquely named rock formations like Hamabawi, Mudangbawi, and Haetaesang throughout the trails. Up at the summit, the broadcasting tower zone—home to KBS and weather observation facilities—blends with the rugged Yeonjudae rock face.

On the valley side, there are spots like the Munwon and Idan waterfalls toward Gwacheon, as well as the valleys near Seoul National University. It’s a great mix, allowing you to enjoy both rugged ridge scrambling and scenic valley hiking.

The Hiking Vibe

The trail starts off gentle, winding through oak and pine forests, but once you push past the mid-section, expect rocks. No matter which route you take, they’re always waiting for you. Once you hit the ridge, it turns into a stretch of granite slabs and rocky ridges, with short scrambles and occasional rope sections to keep you on your toes.

But man, the views make it all worth it. To the north, you get a sweeping view of downtown Seoul and the Han River, with the skylines of Bukhansan and Dobongsan visible in the distance. To the south, Gwacheon and Cheonggyesan stretch out beautifully.

Each season has its own charm: azaleas in the spring, the refreshing coolness of the valleys in summer, vibrant foliage and crisp views in autumn, and stunning snowscapes in winter. Just a heads-up, though—winter can be pretty brutal. Between the ice on the rocks and the biting cold, it’s definitely the most challenging season to tackle.

While the data lists the maximum difficulty as “Moderate,” the real thing to watch out for is that much of the terrain is rocky—that’s just the nature of Gwanaksan. On a dry day, the granite offers great grip, making for a pleasant hike. However, if it’s been raining or if there’s ice, those rock slabs can become incredibly slippery. You’ll need to be especially careful on the stairs and rock-face sections just before Yeonjudae, as well as the rope sections on the Palbong and Jaunam ridges; in winter, you absolutely cannot skip the crampons. There is one officially registered danger zone on the map, and since the ridges are highly exposed to lightning and heavy winds, you should plan to either divert to a valley route or call it a day if the weather takes a turn for the worse.

Distance and Time Estimates

Gwanaksan is a perfectly doable day hike. Based on actual measurements, one-way distances range from 6.37 to 10.83 km, typically taking between 2 hours 31 minutes and 3 hours 39 minutes. For a full round trip, I’d suggest budgeting about 4 to 6 hours. The popular routes, like the Gwacheon Hyanggyo or Seoul National University trails, usually take around 4 to 5 hours.

Now, don’t let that total length of 43.69 km intimidate you—that’s just the sum of all the various trails combined, not a single route. Even if you plan an ambitious ridge trek (like connecting Sadang to Gwacheon or linking up with Samsungsan), you can still typically finish it in a single day. That said, if you’re looking to tackle a longer trek at a more leisurely pace, you might consider splitting it into two days (there is one shelter available in the area).

Getting There (Public Transit & Parking)

  • Subway: Line 2 (Seoul National University or Nakseongdae Stations, toward SNU/Jaunam), Line 4 (Sadang Station for Sadang Ridge; Gwacheon or Government Complex Gwacheon Stations toward Gwacheon Hyanggyo/Munwon-dong), and Line 1 (Gwanak Station toward Anyang Art Park, a common access route). From the station to the trailhead, you can typically expect a 10–30 minute walk or a quick bus transfer.
  • Bus: From Seoul National University or Nakseongdae stations, you can take local feeder buses (such as the 5511 or 5513) toward the SNU campus to reach the trailhead.
  • Parking: There is only one registered parking lot listed, so it isn’t exactly spacious. While there are paid lots near Gwacheon Hyanggyo or SNU, they tend to fill up fast on weekend mornings. I’d strongly suggest sticking to public transit instead.
  • For the most accurate routes and bus numbers, it’s best to check KakaoMap or Naver Map by searching for the specific trailhead name (e.g., “Gwacheon Hyanggyo” or “Gwanaksan Park Entrance”).

Season, Weather, and Gear

  • Best Timing: Spring (April–May, for the azaleas) and Autumn (October–November, for the foliage and clear views) are definitely the sweet spots.
  • Summer: The rocky ridges can get scorching hot, and there isn’t much shade. An early start is a must, as is bringing plenty of water (at least 1.5L). If you run into sudden showers or lightning, get off the ridges immediately.
  • Winter: This is arguably the toughest season due to icy rock surfaces. Crampons (spikes) are non-negotiable, and you’ll definitely want a windbreaker and warm gloves.
  • Basic Gear: Stick to hiking boots with good grip—I wouldn’t recommend sneakers given the rocky terrain. You’ll also need water, some trail snacks, a headlamp (just in case you’re out past sunset), and trekking poles to protect your knees on the way down. Since spring and autumn can have those sudden temperature swings, make sure to pack some extra layers.

Tips for Foreign Travelers

  • Place Names: Gwanaksan, Yeonjudae (the summit), Sadang Station, Gwacheon Hyanggyo, Seoul National University, Nakseongdae Station, Palbong Ridge.
  • Permits & Fees: No permits or entrance fees are required, and you can hike freely without making a reservation. Just a heads-up: I wouldn’t personally recommend night hiking for safety reasons.
  • Restrooms & Water: Public restrooms are available at the main trailheads (Gwanaksan Park, Seoul National University, and Gwacheon Hyanggyo), with some simple pit toilets scattered along the trails. There are seven registered water fountains/springs where you can refill, but since water quality can fluctuate depending on the season, I’d suggest sticking to bottled water just to be safe. If you take the Gwacheon course, there is also a small shop (Gwanaksan-jang) to grab some supplies.
  • Map Apps: A quick tip—Google Maps isn’t very reliable for Korean hiking trails. I recommend installing KakaoMap or Naver Map (both have English support), and it’s also a good idea to use a GPX-compatible app like Ramblr or AllTrails for more detailed pathing. There are plenty of forks in the trail, so keeping an eye on your app is pretty much essential.
  • Safety: In an emergency, dial 119 (they can connect you to English interpreters). If you do need help, use the location signposts (National Location Numbers) to tell them exactly where you are. When you hit rocky sections, please stick to the designated trails, and in winter, definitely don’t attempt rock scrambles without proper gear. Since this is a “real rocky mountain” right in the heart of Seoul, please come prepared for a proper hike rather than treating it like a casual stroll.

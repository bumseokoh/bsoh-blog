Overview

Gwanaksan (관악산, 冠岳山) is a rocky mountain of roughly 632m straddling Seoul’s Gwanak-gu and Geumcheon-gu districts and the cities of Gwacheon and Anyang in Gyeonggi Province — though according → measured course data, the highest point around the summit area actually comes in at 657m. At the summit, Yeonjudae, a small hermitage (Eungjinjeon) perches on →p of a sheer rock cliff, giving it what might be the most dramatic summit view of any mountain near Seoul. The trail data rates the hardest difficulty as “moderate,” and the →tal length of trails within the area adds up → 43.69km (that’s the sum of multiple courses, mind you, not a single trail). With trailheads reachable straight from subway stations, it’s a mountain you can easily knock out as a day hike.

Terrain and Ridgelines

Gwanaksan is a classic granite rock mountain. Granite peaks and slabs (sloping rock faces) are exposed all along the ridges, and even the trail-surface data records the main surface type as “rock.” Its signature summits are Yeonjudae at the →p and the neighboring peak with the weather-radar dome, while the Palbong Ridge — eight rocky peaks strung →gether like saw teeth — runs → the west, and the Hakbawi and Jaunam ridges stretch → the northwest. This is a mountain full of oddly shaped rocks: public data registers scenic viewpoint rocks like Saebawi, Madangbawi, and Geobukbawi as waypoints, and the actual trail signs feature a whole parade of named rocks — Hamabawi, Beoseotbawi, Eomjibawi, Tokkibawi, and more. As for valleys, the most notable are the Munwon Falls and Idan Falls valley on the Gwacheon side and the valley on the Seoul National University side.

What the Hike Feels Like

The trail near the entrance starts gently through an oak forest, but once you pass the midpoint, rocky sections quickly take over. Up on the ridge, the views open right up — down→wn Seoul and the Han River, with Cheonggyesan and the city of Gwacheon spread out → the south. The vistas come often enough that public data lists no fewer than nine registered viewpoints along the way. With a mix of hands-on scrambles and rope sections, the hike feels considerably →ugher than the distance would suggest. In spring you get cherry blossoms and royal azaleas at the Gwanaksan Park entrance; in autumn, the fall colors set against the rocky ridgeline are lovely; and in winter, snow resting on the rocks makes for a beautiful scene — though the risk of ice goes up considerably.

On paper, the hardest rating here is “moderate,” but with so much of the terrain being bare rock, it’ll feel →ugher than the numbers suggest. The Palbong Ridge, Jaunam Ridge, and the staircase just below Yeonjudae in particular get genuinely dangerous in winter — don’t even think about them without crampons when things ice over — and when it rains, the granite slabs turn slick and accidents are common. The public data lists 1 hazard zone, so if you see that warning sign, take the de→ur. As for facilities, there are 9 viewpoints (Saebawi, Madangbawi, Geobukbawi, a watch→wer with a T.P. benchmark, a bunker and helipad, a lookout post, a rest area with a view, and so on), 7 drinking fountains (including natural springs), 1 shelter (the spot with the wildfire notice board, PA equipment, and a forest interpretation sign), and 1 parking lot.

Distance and Time Required

Gwanaksan is very much a day-hike mountain. On the main routes, figure roughly 2.5 → 3 hours 40 minutes one way, or about 5–7 hours round trip. The data shows a cumulative →tal of 779 minutes ascending and 542 minutes descending across all sections in the park — but that’s the sum of many separate segments, so don’t mistake it for the time of a single route. That said, the average ascending pace of about 18 minutes per kilometer you can derive from it is a handy benchmark for planning your own hike. Only if you’re aiming → traverse the full 43.69 km of trails across multiple ridges would an overnight even come in→ play (there’s one shelter in the park) — a standard out-and-back → the summit is easily done in a day.

Getting There (Public Transit & Parking)

  • Subway: The main gateways are Nakseongdae, Seoul Nat’l Univ., and Sillim stations on Line 2 (Seoul side), and Sadang, Gwacheon, and Government Complex Gwacheon stations on Line 4 (Gwacheon side). From Seoul Nat’l Univ. Station, buses like the 5511 and 5513 connect you → the trailheads at the Seoul National University campus.
  • Bus: City and neighborhood buses run → s→ps near each trailhead, but the data shows no registered bus s→ps right at the trail entrances, so plan on walking from the station as your default.
  • Parking: According → public data, there’s only one parking lot along the course. There are paid lots on the Gwacheon Hyanggyo and Seoul National University sides, but they get extremely crowded on weekends — I’d strongly recommend public transit.
  • For actual routes and bus numbers, your best bet is → punch your starting point in→ KakaoMap or Naver Map.

Seasons, Weather, and Gear

  • Best seasons: Spring (April–May) and fall (Oc→ber–November) are ideal. In spring you get cherry blossoms at the Gwanaksan Park entrance, and in fall the foliage along the rocky ridges is gorgeous.
  • Summer: The granite gets scorching hot and there are long slab sections with zero shade, so bring plenty of water (at least 1.5L) and start early in the morning.
  • Winter: The rocks ice over badly, so crampons (chain spikes) and trekking poles are practically manda→ry — and pack windproof layers.
  • Year-round essentials: Hiking boots with good grip (sneakers not recommended — it’s rocky terrain), gloves for the rock sections, plenty of water, snacks, and a power bank. If it rains, skip the ridge routes and switch → the gentler valley-side trails — it’s the safer call.

Tips for International Travelers

  • Key place names in Roman letters: Gwanaksan (Mt. Gwanak), the summit Yeonjudae, Nakseongdae Station, Sadang Station, Gwacheon Station, Seoul National University (SNU), Palbong Ridge.
  • Permits & entrance fees: There’s no permit or entrance fee — it’s free, and no reservation is needed. That said, do respect the nighttime hiking restrictions and the closed-off side trails (they’re marked with official signs).
  • Restrooms & drinking water: There are several public and portable restrooms at the trailheads and along the early sections, and seven registered spring-water points along the routes. There’s nothing up on the ridge, though, so fill up your water before heading up. The Gwacheon Hyanggyo route even has a small shop (Gwanaksan Sanjang).
  • Map apps: Google Maps is unreliable for Korean hiking trails and walking routes. Make sure you install KakaoMap or Naver Map (both support English). This mountain has a lot of forks in the trail, so get in the habit of checking your GPS.
  • Safety: Take note of the location marker signs (multi-purpose location numbers) posted along the trails — they’re useful if you ever need → call 119 in an emergency. Some of the viewpoints along the routes also have emergency contact signs. On the rocky sections, stick → the designated rope and stairway routes, and skip the rock-ridge courses when it’s raining or icy.

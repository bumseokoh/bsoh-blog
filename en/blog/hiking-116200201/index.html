Sawtooth Palbong Ridge and the Rock Spires of Yeonjudae: Seoul's Gwanaksan

개요

Gwanaksan (冠岳山) is a 632-meter-class rocky peak that straddles the Gwanak and Geumcheon districts of Seoul and the cities of Gwacheon and Anyang in Gyeonggi Province. According to measured course data, the highest point around the summit actually reaches 657 meters. At the top sits Yeonjudae, a small hermitage (Eungjinjeon) perched on a dramatic outcrop of rock — which gives it arguably the most striking summit view of any mountain near Seoul. The trail data rates the toughest sections as “moderate,” and the mountain’s trails add up to 43.69 km in total (that’s the sum of several courses, not the distance of any single one). Best of all, the trailhead connects straight from a subway station, so it’s easily doable as a day hike.

Mountain Character and Terrain

Gwanaksan is a classic granite peak. Granite crags and slabs (sloping rock faces) break through the surface all along the ridgelines, and even the trail-surface data lists the main footing as “rock.” The signature summits are Yeonjudae at the very top and the neighboring peak crowned with a weather-radar dome. To the west runs Palbong Ridge, where eight rocky peaks line up like the teeth of a saw, and to the northwest stretch Hakbawi Ridge and Jaunam Ridge. Public datasets register lookout rocks like Saebawi, Madangbawi, and Geobukbawi as named points, and the on-the-ground trail markers list one named rock after another — Hamabawi, Beoseotbawi, Eomjibawi, Tokkibawi — so this is a mountain absolutely full of oddly shaped rocks. As for valleys, the standouts are the Munwon Falls and Idan Falls valley on the Gwacheon side and the valley on the Seoul National University side.

What the Hike Feels Like

The trailhead starts off gently through an oak forest, but once you get past the halfway point, the rocky sections quickly take over. Reach the ridgeline and Seoul spreads out below you — the city, the Han River, and to the south, Cheonggyesan and the town of Gwacheon opening up wide. Views like this come often; public data lists nine registered viewpoints alone. The trail mixes stretches where you scramble up rock on all fours with rope sections, so it feels tougher than the distance would suggest. In spring, there are cherry blossoms and royal azaleas near the entrance to Gwanaksan Park; in autumn, the fall foliage plays beautifully against the rocky ridges; and in winter, snow settling on the rocks makes for gorgeous scenery — though the risk of ice goes way up.

Course 1 — Inheon High School Trailhead → Gwanaksan Park Scenic Overlook

📏 10.83km ⛰️ 570m gain ⏱️ 3:39 ➡️ One-way Difficulty ★★★★★ ⤓ GPX
  • Route: Inheon High School trailhead → junction → path toward the Gwaneumsa flagpole → Inheon-dong view → Gwanaksan Park scenic overlook → closed side trail → Seonyucheon flagpole → path toward Yeonjudae → Hamabawi Rock → Haetae Statue → just before the helipad → below Yeonjudae → mid-staircase before reaching Yeonjudae → Gwanaksan Yeonjudae → just below Yeonjudae
  • Getting to the trailhead: The usual approach is to head toward Inheon-dong by village bus or on foot from Nakseongdae Station (Subway Line 2) or Sadang Station (Line 4). Since the trailhead sits in a residential neighborhood, there’s no dedicated parking lot, so I’d recommend public transit.
  • What it’s like / who it’s for: This is the longest all-in-one course, running past the Gwaneumsa flagpole and the scenic overlook before connecting to the Sadang ridge, with views over central Seoul following you the whole way. Both the distance and the rocky ridges are long, so it suits intermediate or more experienced hikers who are confident in their stamina.

Course 2 — Faculty Club Entrance → Summit

  • Waypoints: Seoul National University → public restroom → trail fork → table bench → wooden platform, bench → wooden platform, bench → table bench, wooden platform → pavilion → portable restroom → viewpoint → Mushroom Rock location sign → Satgatseunggun location sign → viewpoint → turn right → location sign, Gwanangmun (upper)
  • Getting to the trailhead: The popular approach is to take Line 2 to Seoul National University Station and catch a campus-bound bus (5511, 5513, and others) that runs deep into the campus. Parking on the SNU campus is paid and can get crowded.
  • What it’s like / who it’s for: This one has a clear change of pace — it starts as a gentle forest path well stocked with benches and restrooms, then turns into a rocky scramble around Mushroom Rock and Gwanangmun. With so many facilities along the way, it’s a good fit for anyone who wants to take it slow and easy on the climb.

Course 3 — Trailhead → Palbong Summit

📏 7.71km ⛰️ 567m gain ⏱️ 2:52 ➡️ One-way Difficulty ★★★★☆ ⤓ GPX
  • Route: Trailhead → Bogwangsa Temple → junction → boxwood grove → rest area toward Madangbawi → Idan Falls → Munwon Falls → Huinmoe-gil → toward Palbong → toward Yukbong → Palbong summit → near the merge point → Yeonjudae-bound main ridge → KBS relay station → intersection
  • Getting to the trailhead: Most people approach from Government Complex Gwacheon Station or Gwacheon Station on Line 4, heading toward Munwon-dong. There’s a public parking lot on the Gwacheon side you can use.
  • What it’s like / who it’s for: This is one of Gwanaksan’s signature rocky-ridge courses — you pass through the Idan Falls and Munwon Falls valley, then climb over eight rock peaks one after another. There are plenty of stretches where you’re using both hands and feet, so it suits experienced hikers who want a thrilling scramble. Beginners, and anyone caught in the rain, are better off avoiding it.

Course 4 — Nadeuli Forest Restroom → Hakbawi Flag Peak

📏 8.92km ⛰️ 579m gain ⏱️ 3:12 🔁 Loop Difficulty ★★★★★ ⤓ GPX
  • Route: Nadeuli Forest restroom → fork → rest area → location sign → spring → viewpoint → rope section → the striking rock → Hakbawi Flag Peak → helipad → Rabbit Rock → Silent Face Rock → 3 table benches → trail exit → bus stop
  • Getting to the trailhead: Most people come in on a bus toward Seoul National University from Sillim Station or Seoul National Univ. Station on Line 2. The trail lets out right at a bus stop, so public transit connections are excellent.
  • Highlights & who it’s for: You top off your water at the spring, work through the rope section, and are rewarded with the Korean flag and the views at Hakbawi Flag Peak. It’s a good fit if you’d rather aim for a quiet ridge peak instead of the main Yeonjudae summit, or if you enjoy hunting down oddly shaped rocks that each have a name.

  • Getting to the trailhead: You walk straight off Line 2’s Nakseongdae Station and you’re on the trail — it’s about as easy as subway-linked hikes get. Parking near the station is a pain, so I’d really recommend taking the subway.
  • What it’s like / who it’s for: This is the Jaunam Ridge route, where you hop from the station right onto the mountain. The real treat is the views from atop the granite slabs like Thumb Rock and Fist Rock. There are an unusual number of forks along the way, so a map app is a must. It suits Seoul-area office workers who want a short but intense rock scramble.

Course 6 — Restaurant at the Entrance → Summit

📏 6.78km ⛰️ 579m gain ⏱️ 2:40 ➡️ One-way Difficulty ★★★★☆ ⤓ GPX
  • Waypoints: Subway → Meeting Point → Fork in the trail → Gwanaksan Restroom → Gwanak Mountain Lodge Shop → Gwacheon Hyanggyo → Restaurant at the entrance → Portable restroom before the fork → Unnamed stone pagoda → Mudang Rock Spring → Songhakjeong Pavilion → Start of the ridge → Cheonggyesan and Gwacheon in the background → Wildfire watch post → Bench below the wildfire watch post
  • Getting to the trailhead: The usual approach is to walk from Gwacheon Station on Line 4 toward Gwacheon Hyanggyo, and there’s a public parking lot in downtown Gwacheon you can use.
  • What makes it special / who it’s for: This is the classic, ever-popular route, with the most tightly packed amenities of any — a shop, restrooms, a spring, and a pavilion all lined up one after another. At 6.37km it’s also the shortest course in the table, which makes it the one I’d recommend first to anyone tackling Gwanaksan for the first time, or to visitors from abroad.

On paper the toughest rating here is only “moderate,” but since the main surface is rock, the trails can feel harder than the numbers suggest. The Palbong Ridge, Jaunam Ridge, and the stairway just before Yeonjudae are especially dangerous — in winter they ice over and you really shouldn’t tackle them without crampons, and in the rain the granite slabs get slick, so accidents are common. Public data lists 1 hazard area, so follow the detour whenever you spot the marker for it. As for facilities, there are 9 viewpoints registered (Saebawi, Madangbawi, Geobukbawi, the watchtower/T.P. reference stone, the bunker/helipad, a monitoring post, a viewpoint attached to a rest area, and so on), 7 water fountains (springs included), 1 shelter (a spot that also has a wildfire-awareness board, broadcasting equipment, and a forest interpretation panel), and 1 parking lot.

Distance and Time Required

Gwanaksan is an easy day hike. On the main routes, one-way takes roughly 2 hours 30 minutes to 3 hours 40 minutes, so figure on about 5 to 7 hours round trip. The data shows a cumulative time of 779 minutes going up and 542 minutes coming down across all the sections within the park, but that’s the sum of many segments combined — don’t mistake it for the time of any single route. That said, the average ascent pace of about 18 minutes per km that this works out to is a handy benchmark for planning your own trip. Only if you’re aiming to traverse the full 43.69 km by linking several ridgelines would you need to consider spending a night or more (there’s one shelter in the park); for a standard round trip to the summit and back, a single day is plenty.

가는 길(대중교통·주차)

  • Subway: The main gateways are Line 2 (Nakseongdae, Seoul National University, and Sillim stations on the Seoul side) and Line 4 (Sadang, Gwacheon, and Government Complex Gwacheon stations on the Gwacheon side). From Seoul National University Station, buses like the 5511 and 5513 connect you to the entrance of the SNU campus trailhead.
  • Bus: City and community buses run to stops near each trailhead, but the data shows no bus stops registered right at the trails themselves, so plan on walking in from the station as your default.
  • Parking: Public data lists only one parking lot along the course. There are paid lots over by Gwacheon Hyanggyo and SNU, but weekends get seriously crowded, so I’d strongly recommend public transit.
  • For the actual route and bus numbers, your best bet is to punch in your starting point on KakaoMap or Naver Map — that’ll be the most accurate.

시즌·날씨·장비

  • Best seasons: Spring (April–May) and fall (October–November) are ideal. In spring you get the cherry blossoms at the Gwanaksan Park entrance, and in fall the foliage along the rocky ridges is gorgeous.
  • Summer: The granite heats up fast and there are plenty of exposed slab sections with no shade, so pack plenty of water (at least 1.5L) and I’d suggest hitting the trail early in the morning.
  • Winter: The rocks ice over badly, so crampons (chain spikes) and trekking poles are basically essential — and bring windproof clothing.
  • Gear for any season: Hiking boots with good grip (skip the sneakers — you’re on rock the whole way), gloves (for the rocky stretches), plenty of water, snacks, and a power bank. If it rains, it’s safer to avoid the ridge route and switch to the gentler path along the valley.

Tips for International Travelers

  • Romanized place names: Gwanaksan (Mt. Gwanak), Yeonjudae (the summit peak), Nakseongdae Station, Sadang Station, Gwacheon Station, Seoul National University (SNU), Palbong Ridge.
  • Permits & fees: There’s no permit required to hike and no entrance fee. It’s free, and you don’t need to book anything in advance. That said, do respect the nighttime hiking restrictions and any closed side trails (they’re clearly marked with official signs).
  • Restrooms & water: There are several public and portable toilets at the trailheads and along the early stretches, and the course has 7 registered water points (spring fountains). Up on the ridge, though, there are no facilities, so fill up your water before you head up. The Gwacheon Hyanggyo route also has a shop (Gwanaksan Sanjang).
  • Map apps: Google Maps is unreliable for Korean hiking trails and walking routes. Be sure to install KakaoMap or Naver Map (which has English support). This mountain has a lot of forks in the trail, so get in the habit of checking your GPS.
  • Safety: It helps to note the location signs (multi-purpose location numbers) posted along the trail — they’re useful when calling 119 in an emergency. Some of the viewpoints along the way also have emergency contact signs. On the rocky sections, stick to the designated ropes and stairs, and avoid the exposed ridge routes in rain or ice.

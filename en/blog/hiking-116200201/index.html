Overview

Gwanaksan sits at the southern edge of Seoul, straddling the city’s Gwanak and Geumcheon districts and the Gyeonggi-do cities of Gwacheon and Anyang. Its summit area, around a spot called Yeonjudae, →ps out at roughly 630–660m. According → measured data, the highest point near the summit is recorded at 657m. As one of Seoul’s classic city-fringe mountains, it’s wonderfully easy → reach — you can get → a trailhead (the point where the hike begins) using nothing but the subway and a bus. The mountain as a whole is what people call a “rock mountain,” full of granite peaks and ridgelines, and the data rates its maximum difficulty as “moderate.” Individual courses generally run about 6–11km one way, so a single day is plenty of time → hike up → the summit and back. That said, if you approach it as a traverse linking several courses →gether, the distance grows considerably — and that’s when you’ll need → pace your energy carefully.

Mountain Terrain and Topography

Rather than a smooth, soil-covered mountain, Gwanaksan is really a mountain built around rocky peaks (암봉) and rocky ridgelines (암릉) — granite crags jutting out all over the place. Big and small rock summits string along the ridges, and up →p you’ll find masses of granite that have been weathering for ages, laid bare. From a distance, that gives the mountain its distinctive jagged silhouette.

Here are the main peaks and their elevations. The area around Yeonjudae, which serves as the summit, is recorded in the data somewhere between 631 and 658m — Gwanaksan’s Yeonjudae (658m), the Gwanaksan summit (619m), and several Yeonjudae readings (647m, 643m, 631m) are all clustered densely across the summit zone. Along the ridge that runs off → the west sits the summit of Samseongsan (499m / 503m), along with subsidiary peaks like Budaeyeopbong (375m). As you hike, you’ll come across plenty of named rock formations — the Palbong and Yukbong ridges, Hamabawi, the Haetae statue, Eomjibawi (Thumb Rock), Jumeokbawi (Fist Rock), Tokkibawi (Rabbit Rock) — and it’s exactly this rocky terrain that defines the character of Gwanaksan.

The Feel of the Hike

Gwanaksan is a mountain of sharp contrasts — gentle forest trails at the bot→m, rugged rock ridges up →p. Near the trailhead you walk easy dirt paths that wind through valleys and broadleaf woods, but the higher you climb →ward the ridgeline, the more often you hit stretches of granite, where you find yourself grabbing the rock with your hands or working your way up steps and ropes. Once you’re standing at the summit, Yeonjudae, the view opens up wide — down→wn Seoul, the Han River, and off → the south →ward Cheonggyesan and Gwacheon. Among the mountains close → the city, this one’s a real standout when it comes → the payoff of the view.

The seasons show up clearly here, →o. In spring it’s fresh greenery and azaleas; in autumn, the contrast between the white granite and the fall foliage is especially beautiful. Summer brings the welcome sound of valley streams and pockets of shade, but the rock soaks up the heat and makes it feel even hotter. And in winter, once snow and ice build up on the rock and the steps, things get slick and the difficulty spikes fast — so it pays → be careful.

Getting There (Public Transit & Parking)

Gwanaksan is wonderfully easy → reach by public transit from both the Seoul and Gwacheon sides.

  • Subway: From the Seoul side, the main gateways are Seoul National University Station and Nakseongdae Station on Line 2; from the Gwacheon side, it’s Government Complex Gwacheon Station on Line 4. Nakseongdae Station leads straight → the trailhead for the Jaunam Ridge, and from Seoul National University Station you can hop on a neighborhood shuttle bus →ward campus → reach the Faculty Hall and the SNU trailhead.
  • Bus: Near the trailheads, there are actual bus s→ps at Government Complex Gwacheon Station Exit 6, Exit 5, and Exit 2, which you can use → reach the courses on the Gwacheon side.
  • Check the real route: Google Maps can be unreliable for Korean public transit and hiking trails, so be sure → check your live route from your starting point using KakaoMap or Naver Map.
  • Parking: There’s a parking lot near the Gwanaksan entrance (just one, according → the data), but it gets extremely crowded on weekends and during peak season, so I’d recommend taking the subway or bus if you can.

Season, Weather & Gear

  • Best seasons: Spring (April–May), with all that fresh new green, and autumn (Oc→ber–November), with its foliage and crisp, clear views, are the two sweet spots. Autumn is especially striking — the white granite against the fall colors is something else.
  • Summer: The valley routes stay cool, but heat radiating off the rocks plus the humidity makes it feel a lot hotter than it is, and the rocks get slippery when a shower rolls through. Hike in the morning and keep your water →pped up.
  • Winter: Ice on the rocks and stairs ramps up the difficulty considerably. I’d steer clear of the ridge routes and stick → the maintained trails.
  • Must-have gear: Hiking boots with good grip, plenty of water, trail snacks, and windproof, insulating layers. In winter, crampons and gloves are pretty much essential. There’s a lot of rocky terrain here, so make sure your shoes have soles that hold up well on slick surfaces.

Tips for International Travelers

  • Place names in Roman letters: Gwanaksan (Gwanak Mountain) / Yeonjudae, the summit / Samseongsan (Samseong Mountain) / Seoul National Univ. Station / Nakseongdae Station / Government Complex Gwacheon Station.
  • Permits and entry fees: Gwanaksan is an open mountain on the edge of the city, with no entry permit or admission fee required. That said, certain sections may be closed during wildfire-prevention periods and the like, so check the local signboards.
  • Restrooms and drinking water: There are restrooms at the main trailheads (the Faculty Hall, the Gwacheon side, Nadeuri Forest, and so on), and several spring-water fountains along the way where you can refill. Since these spots are limited, though, I’d suggest packing plenty of water in advance.
  • Map apps: Google Maps can be unreliable for Korean hiking trails and public transit. Install KakaoMap or Naver Map → check your route, and for trail tracking I’d recommend a Korean hiking app.
  • Safety: There’s a lot of exposed rock underfoot, so watch out for slips in wet or icy conditions. With so many lookout points and forks in the trail, check the signposts often, and give yourself plenty of time so you finish your descent before sunset.
